10Newton's Laws of Motion

11In the late 1600s (around 1687), Sir 13Isaac Newton put forth his philosophies on mechanics in his 13Philosophiae Naturalis Principia Mathematica474 . 14 In this work Newton stated the now-famous laws of motion, which are summarized here:

15Law I

479A body tends to remain at rest or continue to move in a straight line at constant velocity unless acted upon by an external force. 480 This is the so-called concept of inertia.

21Law II

484The acceleration of a body is proportional to the resultant force acting on the body, and this acceleration is in the same direction as the resultant force.

25Law III

488For every force acting on a body (action) there is an equal and opposite reacting force (reaction), where the reaction is collinear to the acting force.

491These laws form the basis for much of the analysis in the field of mechanics. 31 Of particular interest to us in the study of dynamics is the second law, which is written:

31F = ma

33where 33F33 is the resultant 34force acting on the body, 34m34 is the 36mass of the body, and 36a36 is the 499linear acceleration of the body's center of gravity. 500 We'll discuss this second law in greater detail later in this chapter, but before that there are some more fundamental issues that we must address.

308Derivatives and Integrals

770If you're not familiar with calculus, or The Calculus, don't let the use of derivatives and integrals in this text worry you. 771 While we'll write equations using derivatives and integrals, we'll show you explicitly how to deal with them computationally throughout this book. 772 Without going into a dissertation on all the properties and applications of derivatives and integrals, let's touch on their physical significance as they relate to the material we'll cover.

313You can think of a 775derivative as the rate of change in one variable with respect to another variable, or in other words, derivatives tells you how fast one variable changes as some other variable changes. 776 Take speed, for example. 777 A car travels at a certain speed covering some distance in a certain period of time. 778 Its speed, on average, is the distance traveled over a specific time interval. 779 If it travels a distance of 60 kilometers in one hour, then its average speed is 60 kilometers an hour. 780 When we're doing simulations, the ones you'll see later in this book, we're interested in what the car is doing over very short time intervals. 320 As the time interval gets really small and we consider the distance traveled over that very short period of time, we're looking at 320instantaneous781 speed. 321 We usually write such relations using symbols like the following:

321|v| = ds/dt

323where 323v323 is the speed, 323ds323 is a small distance (a 323differential323 distance), and 323dt784 is a small, differential, period of time. 785 In reality, for our simulations, we'll never deal with infinitely small numbers; we'll use small numbers, such as time intervals of 1 millisecond, but not infinitely small numbers.

327For our purposes, you can think of 789integrals as the reverse, or the inverse, of derivatives; integration is the inverse of differentiation. 790 The symbol ∫ represents integration. 791 You can think of integration as a process of adding up a bunch of infinitely small chunks of some variable. 792 Here again, we are not going to deal with infinitely small pieces of anything, but instead will consider small, discrete parcels of some variable—for example, a small, discrete amount of time, area, or mass. 793 In these cases, we'll use the ∑ symbol instead of the integration symbol. 794 Consider a loaf of bread that's sliced into uniformly thick slices along its whole length. 795 If you wanted to compute the volume of that loaf of bread, you can approximate it by starting at one end and computing the volume of the first slice, approximating its volume as though it were a very short, square cylinder; then moving on to the second slice, estimating its volume and adding that to the volume of the first slice; and then moving on to the third, and fourth, and so on, aggregating the volume of the loaf as you move toward the other end. 796 Integration applies this technique to infinitely thin slices of volume to compute the volume of any arbitrary shape. 336 The same techniques apply to other computations—for example, computing areas, 336inertias797 , masses, and so on, and even aggregating distance traveled over successive small slices of time, as you'll see later. 798 In fact, this latter application is the inverse of the derivative of distance with respect to time, which gives speed. 799 Using integration and differentiation in this way allows you to work back and forth when computing speed, acceleration, and distance traveled, as you'll see shortly. 800 In fact, we'll use these concepts heavily throughout the rest of this book.

342Mass, Center of Mass, and Moment of Inertia

344The properties of a 345body—345mass345, 345center of mass345, and 345moment of inertia345, collectively called 345mass properties—345are absolutely crucial to the study of mechanics, as the 350linear and angular350[3515813 ] motion of a body and a body's response to a given force are functions of these mass properties. 814 Thus, in order to accurately model a body in motion, you need to know or be capable of calculating these mass properties. 815 Let's look at a few definitions first.

356In general, people think of mass 819as a measure of the amount of matter in a body. 820 For our purposes in the study of mechanics, we can also think of mass as a measure of a body's resistance to motion or a change in its motion. 821 Thus, the greater a body's mass, the harder it will be to set it in motion or change its motion.

363In laymen's terms, the 366center of mass (also known as 366center of gravity827 ) is the point in a body around which the mass of the body is evenly distributed. 828 In mechanics, the center of mass is the point through which any force can act on the body without resulting in a rotation of the body.

370Although most people are familiar with the terms 370mass370 and 370center of gravity370, the term 371moment of inertia833 is not so familiar; however, in mechanics it is equally important. 373 The 836mass moment of inertia of a body is a quantitative measure of the radial distribution of the mass of a body about a given axis of rotation. 376 Analogous to mass being a measure of a body's resistance to linear motion, mass moment of inertia (also known 377as 377rotational inertia839 ) is a measure of a body's resistance to rotational motion.

842Now that you know what these properties mean, let's look at how to calculate each.

384For a given body 847made up of a number of particles, the total mass of the body is simply the sum of the masses of all elemental particles making up the body, where the mass of each elemental particle is its mass density times its volume. 848 Assuming that the body is of uniform density, then the total mass of the body is simply the density of the body times the total volume of the body. 388 This is expressed in the following equation:

388m = ∫ ρ dV = ρ ∫ dV

851In practice, you rarely need to take the volume integral to find the mass of a body, especially considering that many of the bodies we will consider—for example, cars and planes—are not of uniform density. 852 Thus, you will simplify these complicated bodies by breaking them down into an ensemble of component bodies of known or easily calculable mass and simply sum the masses of all components to arrive at the total mass.

744Tensors

746A tensor is a 747mathematical expression that has 1211magnitude and direction, but its magnitude may not be unique depending on the direction. 1212 Tensors are typically used to represent properties of materials where these properties have different magnitudes in different directions. 752 Materials with properties that vary depending on direction are called 752anisotropic 753(753isotropic753 implies 1216the same magnitude in all directions). 1217 For example, consider the elasticity (or strength) of two common materials, a sheet of plain paper and a piece of woven or knitted cloth. 1218 Take the sheet of paper and, holding it flat, pull on it softly from opposing ends. 1219 Try this length-wise, width-wise, and along a diagonal. 1220 You should observe that the paper seems just as strong, or stretches about the same, in all directions. 1221 It is isotropic; therefore, only a single scalar constant is required to represent its strength for all directions.

1224Now, try to find a piece of cloth with a simple, relatively loose weave where the threads in one direction are perpendicular to the threads in the other direction. 1225 Most neckties will do. 1226 Try the same pull test that you conducted with the sheet of paper, pulling the cloth along each thread direction and then at a diagonal to the threads. 1227 You should observe that the cloth stretches more when you pull it along a diagonal to the threads as opposed to pulling it along the direction of the run of the threads. 1228 The cloth is anisotropic in that it exhibits different elastic (or strength) properties depending on the direction of pull; thus, a collection of vector quantities (a tensor) is required to represent its strength for all directions.

1231In the context of this book, the property under consideration is a body's moment of inertia, which in 3D requires nine components to fully describe it for any arbitrary rotation. 771 Moment of inertia is not a 1233strength property as in the paper and cloth example, but it is a property of the body that varies with the axis of rotation. 773 Since nine components are required, moment of inertia will be generalized in the form of a 3×3 matrix (i.e., a 773second-rank1234 tensor) later in this book.

1237We need to mention a few things at this point regarding coordinates, which will become important when you're writing your real-time simulator. 1238 Both of the equations of motion have, so far, been written in terms of global coordinates and not body-fixed coordinates. 1239 That's OK for the linear equation of motion, where you can track the body's location and velocity in the global coordinate system. 1240 However, from a computational point of view, you don't want to do that for the angular equation of motion for bodies that rotate in three dimensions.780[78171243 ] The reason is because the moment of inertia term, when calculated with respect to global coordinates, actually changes depending on the body's position and orientation. 1244 This means that during your simulation you'll have to recalculate the inertia matrix (and its inverse) a lot, which is computationally inefficient. 1245 It's better to rewrite the equations of motion in terms of local (attached to the body) coordinates so you have to calculate the inertia matrix (and its inverse) only once at the start of your simulation.

916Relativistic Time

918To allow for a 1381thorough understanding of how advanced space vehicles work as well as give you a mechanism by which to alter time in your games, we would like to offer a brief introduction to the theory of relativity, and particularly its effect on time. 1382 In our everyday experience, it is safe to assume that the clock on your wall is ticking at the same rate as the clock on our wall as we write this. 922 However, the reason we all know the name 1384Albert Einstein is that he had the foresight to abandon time as a constant. 924 Instead he postulated that 1387light travels at the same speed regardless of the motion of the source.

929That is to say, if you shine a flashlight in a vacuum, the electromagnetic radiation it emits in the form of visible light travels at a set velocity of 929c1390 (299,792,458 m/s). 930 Now, if you take that same flashlight and put it on the nose of a rocket traveling at half that speed directly at you, you might expect that light is traveling at you with a velocity of 1.5930c1391 . 931 Yet, the rocket-powered flashlight would still be observed as emitting light at a velocity of 931c1392 . 932 As Einstein's theory of special relativity matured, the postulate has been reformulated to state that there is a maximum speed at which information can be transferred in the space-time continuum, a principal 933called 933locality1395 . 935 As electromagnetic radiation has 936no mass,936[93881399 ] it travels at this maximum speed in a vacuum.

1401The most startling consequence of the theory is that time is no longer absolute. 941 The postulate that the speed of light is constant for all frames of reference requires that time slow down, or 941dilate1402 , as velocity increases. 1403 It is actually fairly easy to demonstrate this result.

1405The following example depicts a conceptual clock. 1406 A beam of light is bouncing between two mirrors. 1407 The time it takes for the beam of light to start from one mirror, bounce off the second, and return to the first constitutes one 1408 "tick" 1409 of this clock. 949 That tick can be calculated as:

949Δt = 2L/c

1437If this isn't clear, we can also come to the same conclusion a different way. 977 If we define the speed of light as the amount of time it takes for the light beam to travel the distance between the mirrors divided by the time it took to travel that distance, we see that:

1465For velocities approaching the speed of light, the effect of time dilation is dramatic. 1466 Imagine if you had a twin sister. 1467 She boards a spaceship and is accelerated to three-fourths the speed of light relative to you on Earth. 1468 Upon her return, according to her clock, 20 years have elapsed since she left. 1469 However, due to time dilation you will have aged 30 years. 1470 While this seems paradoxical given that you both will have observed each other's time as running slowly compared to your own, the paradox is resolved by the fact that special relativity claims only that inertial frames of reference are identical. 1471 For the spaceship to return to Earth, it must accelerate by changing directions or, in other words, become a noninertial frame of reference. 1472 Once the ship is no longer an inertial frame of reference, there will appear to be a jump in the stay-at-home twin's age. 1473 Strange but true!

1476In addition to time dilation due to relative velocity, time also slows down in the presence of strong gravitational fields as a result of Einstein's theory of general relativity. 1477 This sort of time dilation is not relative in the sense that if I were closer to a black hole than you, we'd both agree that my clock is ticking less often than yours. 1478 However, given that all physical processes would be slower, there is no way to establish which clock is 1479 "faster" 1480 and which is 1481 "slower." 1482 It is all relative!

1485As some games require a way to speed up or slow down time, the applications of time dilation allow for a physical phenomenon to enable time manipulation. 1486 Imagine your character needing to be sent to the future to complete some task. 1487 That character could be put into a centrifuge and accelerated near the speed of light. 1488 Upon exiting the centrifuge, he would essentially have time-traveled into the future. 1489 However, if you plan to stay within the limits of physics, then it is a one-way trip: there is no way to reverse time in relativity! 1490 Additionally, if you would like to send a character to a nearby star, you can do so within the limits of physics as well. 1491 By accelerating a spaceship, a human being would be able to travel a great distance in a lifetime. 1031 In fact, with a constant acceleration of 9.8 m/s103121492 , which would make the astronauts feel like they were on Earth, you could travel the entire visible galaxy. 1493 However, you would have essentially time-traveled billions of years into the future. 1494 We are sure that you can imagine any number of scenarios where such a mechanism might make your game more interesting while always remaining in the realm of the physically possible!

1497Now, besides those implications to games involving space flight or high-velocity travel, time dilation is also important to some surprising digital electronic applications. 1037 For instance, the Global Positioning System (GPS), described in detail in 1038Chapter 221500 , must take relativistic time dilation into account when calculating position. 1501 The satellite's high speed slows the clock compared to your watch on Earth; however, being farther up the Earth's gravity causes it to tick faster than a terrestrial clock. 1041 The specifics of this combined effect are discussed in 1042Chapter 221504 .
