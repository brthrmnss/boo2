1Chapter 16. Ships and Boats

2The physics of 8729ships is a vast subject. 8730 While the same principles govern canoes and super tankers, the difference between the two scales is not trivial. 8731 Our goal in this chapter will be to explain some of the fundamental physical principles to allow you to develop realistic simulations. 8732 The typical displacement-type ship lends itself well to illustrating these principles; however, many of these principles also apply to other objects submerged or partially submerged in a fluid, such as submarines and air balloons. 8733 Remember, air is considered a fluid when we are considering buoyancy.

8736While surface ships or ships that operate on the water's surface (at the air water interface) are similar to fully submerged objects like submarines or air balloons in that they all experience buoyancy, there are some very distinct differences in their physical nature that we'll highlight in this chapter. 8737 These differences affect their behavior, so it is important to be aware of them if you intend to simulate such objects.

8740Ships have an entire language of their own, so we'll be spending a lot of time just getting the vocabulary right. 8741 This will allow you to do further research on any topics that are of particular interest. 17 There are many ways to classify ships and boats, but in regards to the physics governing them, there are three basic 8743types. 19 19Displacement19 vessels, 19semi-displacement19 vessels, and 19planing8744 vessels are named after the forces that keep the boat afloat while it is at cruising speed. 20 When not moving, all vessels are in 20displacement mode8745 .

23The term 23displacement23 in 8749this context means that the ship is supported solely by buoyancy—that is, without dynamic or aerostatic lift as you would see on a high-speed racing boat or a hovercraft. 25 The word 25displacement8750 itself refers to the volume of water displaced or 8751 "pushed" 8752 out of the way by the ship as it sits floating in the water. 8753 We'll discuss this more in the next section.

31A planing vessel is 32one that is not supported by buoyancy, but by 8759hydrodynamic lift. 8760 This includes the everyday speedboats that most boaters own. 8761 When the boat isn't moving, it just floats in the water, bobbing up and down. 8762 However, when the boat begins traveling at high speed, the force of the water hitting the bottom of the boat causes the boat to rise up. 8763 This is known as planing, and it greatly reduces the resistance of the vessel. 8764 Semi-displacement vessels are those that straddle the two categories, with some support coming from buoyancy and some coming from planing forces. 8765 Before we continue discussing this, let's go over some vocabulary.

72Stability and Sinking

8799If you have boats in your video game, the first step to making them realistic physically is allowing them to sink if they become damaged. 75 To understand why boats sink and how they do so, you must first understand 75stability8800 .

78Stability

80Most boats 8807are least stable about their longitudinal axis—that is, they are easier to heel port and starboard than they are to flip end over end. 8808 If the vessel heels over so far that it is upside down, this is called capsizing. 84 8810This is how most boats sink due to wind, waves, or in some cases of side damage. 86 One of the most famous examples of a sinking ship, the 86Titanic8811 , shows that when a boat is sinking from damage, it can sink end over end, sometimes with the ship breaking in two. 8812 We'll discuss both here so that you can animate realistic sinking in your simulation.

90In 91Chapter 391 we introduced the concept of buoyancy and stated that the force on a submerged object due to buoyancy is a function of the submerged 8819volume of the object. 95 97Archimedes's principle states that the 8824weight of an object floating in a fluid is equal to the weight of the volume of fluid displaced by the object. 8825 This is an important principle. 8826 It says that a ship of a given weight must have sufficient volume to displace enough water, an amount equal to the weight of the ship, in order for it to float. 8827 Further, this principle provides a clever way of determining the weight of a ship: simply measure or calculate the amount of water displaced by the ship and you can calculate the weight of the ship. 103 In the marine field, 8829displacement is synonymous with the weight of the ship.

107As discussed in 108Chapter 3108, we can calculate the buoyant force on any object by using the following formula:

108F108B108 = ρ g ∇

110Here, ∇ is the submerged volume of the object, ρ is the density of the fluid within which the object is submerged, and 110g8835 is the acceleration due to gravity. 8836 Since buoyancy is a force, it has both magnitude and direction, and always acts straight up through the center of buoyancy. 8837 The center of buoyancy is the geometric center of the submerged part of the object.

114When a ship is floating in equilibrium on the surface of the water, its center of buoyancy must be located directly below the ship's 8840center of gravity. 8841 The weight of the ship, a force, acts straight down through the center of gravity, opposing the force due to buoyancy. 8842 When the ship is in equilibrium, these two forces, weight and buoyancy, are equal in magnitude and opposite in direction.

120Now, when an external force causes the ship to 8847roll or pitch, the portion of the hull below the water is changed and the center of buoyancy moves to the new geometric centroid of the underwater portion of the hull. 8848 For example, if the ship rolls to the starboard side, then the center of buoyancy shiwfts out toward the starboard side. 8849 When this happens, the lines of action of the weight of the ship and the buoyant force are no longer in line, which results in a moment (torque) that acts on the ship. 8850 This torque is equal to the perpendicular distance between the lines of action of the forces times the weight of the ship.

8853Now here's where we get to the floating upright part that we mentioned earlier. 8854 When a ship rolls, for example, you don't want it to keep rolling until it capsizes. 8855 Instead, you want it to gently return itself to the upright position after whatever force caused it to roll—the wind, for example—has been removed. 8856 In short, you want the ship to be stable. 132 For a ship to be stable, the line of action of the buoyant force must cross the vessel's centerline at a point, called 133the 133metacenter8859 , above the center of gravity. 8860 When this happens, the moment developed when the ship rolls tends to restore the ship to the upright position. 8861 If the metacenter is located below the center of gravity, then the moment developed would tend to capsize the ship. 137 The distance between the center of gravity and the metacenter is called 137GM8862 . 138 This is also known as 140the 140stability index8865 , as a positive value means the floating body is stable and a negative GM means the body is unstable. 141 142Figure 16-28868 illustrates these two scenarios.

149Figure 16-2. Ship stability

8876If you're a sailor, then you know how important it is to keep the center of gravity of your boat low. 8877 This helps increase the height of the metacenter above the center of gravity, and thus helps with stability.

8880In the case of fully submerged objects, like submarines, the situation is different. 8881 The buoyant force still acts through the geometric centroid of the object, but for stability, the center of buoyancy must be located above the center of gravity. 8882 This way, when the object rotates, the lines of action of the weight of the object and the buoyant force are separated and form a moment that tends to restore the object to its upright position. 8883 If it's the other way around, then the object would be unstable, like trying to balance one bowling ball on top of another. 159 In this case, the slightest disturbance would upset the balance and the object would flip upside-down such that the center of gravity is located below the center of 8886buoyancy.

164Sinking

166In general, 168boats protect their stability by compartmentalizing the hull into several watertight sections, fittingly 169called 169compartments8895 . 8896 This way, if the side of a vessel hits an iceberg, only the compartment damaged will flood with seawater. 8897 If enough compartments are damaged, the vessel will not have enough buoyancy to support its weight and it will sink. 8898 The end with the flooded compartments will sink first, causing a large angle about the transverse axis. 174 This is what happened to the 174Titanic8899 . 175 In fact, in that ship's case, the angle, called 175trim8900 , was so large that the stern was lifted out of the water. 8901 The hull could not support the weight of the stern section that was no longer being supported by buoyancy, and the structure ripped in two.

8904It should be noted that ships can sink in the matter of minutes, or it can take hours. 180 For instance, the 180Titanic8905 took about three hours to sink. 181 The 181Lusitania8906 sank in 18 minutes. 8907 The time it takes depends heavily on the type of damage and the construction of the vessel. 8908 We don't suggest trying to get players to wait three hours for their game to end; however, it is possible to continue fighting/propelling a vessel that is terminally damaged. 8909 In many cases where terminal damage is suspected, captains endeavor to ground their vessels to prevent the ship from actually going under.

8912If side damage occurs, especially in high wind and waves, then it could be that the vessel can still have enough buoyancy to float, but no longer enough stability to remain upright. 8913 As damage usually occurs only on one side of a vessel, the center of buoyancy will no longer be on centerline. 8914 This means that the restoring moment in one direction is diminished by whatever amount the center has moved to that side. 8915 A big wave comes along and pushes the vessel over to the point where the righting arm is no longer positive. 191 The vessel will flip 180 degrees with the bottom pointed skyward but will still 8917float (capsizing). 8918 Once rolled over, the remaining compartments will tend to fill with water as vents or other openings fail over time. 8919 In the case of recreational boats, they are usually only a single compartment. 8920 If they capsize, they will sink readily; indeed, this is the way that most small boats sink.

198As we mentioned before, accurately computing all degrees 8924of freedom for a nontrivial-shaped body in real time would be difficult to accomplish with today's computer hardware. 200 In general, you want to follow a few high-level rules:

  • 8928The higher the center of gravity, the more likely it is that the boat will tip over.

  • 8931Large vessels are always compartmentalized. 8932 Damage should be limited to the watertight compartment in which it occurred.

  • 8935The vessel will heal or trim in the direction of damage. 8936 If damage occurs on the starboard side, the boat will heel to starboard. 8937 If the damage occurs in the bow, the boat will list forward.

  • 8940A boat will remain floating as long as the undamaged compartments have a volume in cubic meters of at least the weight of the hull in metric tons divided by 1.025.

  • 8943After being damaged, even if a vessel has enough undamaged volume to remain afloat, it doesn't necessarily mean it will float upright.

  • 8946Sinking almost never occurs as quickly as depicted in video games; however, capsizing can occur rapidly and is probably a more realistic way to model a stability failure.

245Roll, pitch, 8973and yaw are the terms also used for airplanes. 8974 The translation degrees of freedom are called surge, heave, and sway. 8975 Surge, sway, and yaw are not that apparent when vessels are moving forward, so it is acceptable to limit your model to heave, pitch, and roll. 251 8977Heave is the up-and-down motion of the boat caused by the change in elevation of the water's surface as a wave passes. 8978 If a vessel is stationary, it would be referred to as bobbing. 8979 Pitch is the rotation about the transverse axis of the vessel due to increased buoyancy on one end of the ship as a wave passes. 8980 This motion is most pronounced when the waves are traveling in the same direction (or 180 degrees) from the vessel. 8981 Roll is like pitch, but about the longitudinal axis.

311Coupled Motions

313The real trick to 9040getting motions to look right is to understand that for most boats the motions are coupled. 9041 For instance, heave and pitch are strongly coupled. 9042 This means that if a wave causes your boat to heave, it will most likely also cause it to pitch. 9043 This is because the distribution of buoyancy along the hull is not constant. 9044 If the waterline is raised some constant value, there is usually more buoyancy provided aft than there is forward for the same change in waterline. 9045 This will cause the vessel to pitch forward to move the center of buoyancy to the longitudinal location of the center of gravity. 321 Similarly, a pitch event will cause the vessel to heave because if the stern is lifted from the water, there is usually a loss of total buoyancy, and the boat will sink farther into the 9048water.

326Resistance and Propulsion

9053Resistance is the amount of force it takes to move a body through the water. 9054 Propulsion is the method by which you create that force.

375The pressure drag 376experienced by a ship is the same as that experienced by projectiles as discussed in 378Chapter 39103 . 9104 Remember, this drag is due to the viscous effects causing a region of relatively low pressure behind the ship. 9105 Quantifying this force is difficult for ships of arbitrary geometry. 9106 We can use computational fluid dynamics algorithms to approximate this force, but this requires detailed knowledge of the hull geometry and a whole lot of time-consuming computations. 9107 An alternative is to rely on scale-model test data where results from the model test are extrapolated to approximate drag on the full-size ship.

466Virtual mass

468The 470concept of 470virtual mass9195 is important for calculating the acceleration of a ship in a real-time simulator. 9196 Virtual mass is equal to the mass of the ship plus the mass of the water that is accelerated with the ship.

474Back in 475Chapter 3475 we told you about the viscous boundary layer, 9203and we said that the relative velocity (relative to the moving body) of the fluid particles near the moving body's surface is 0 at the body surface and increases to the free stream velocity as distance from the body surface increases. 9204 Essentially, some of the fluid sticks to the body as it moves and is accelerated with the body. 9205 Since the velocity of the fluid varies within the boundary layer, so does the acceleration. 481 The 481added mass9206 , the mass of water that gets accelerated, is a weighted integration of the entire mass of fluid that is affected by the body's acceleration.

9209For a ship, the viscous boundary layer can be quite thick, up to several feet near the end of the ship depending on its length, and the mass of water that gets accelerated is significant. 9210 Therefore, when doing any sort of analysis that involves the acceleration of the ship, you need to consider added mass, too. 9211 The calculation of added mass is beyond the scope of this book. 9212 We should also point out that, unlike mass, added mass is a tensor—that is, it depends on the direction of acceleration. 9213 Further, added mass applies to both linear and angular motion.

9216Added mass is typically expressed in terms of an added mass coefficient, which equals the added mass divided by the mass of the ship. 9217 Some methods actually integrate over the actual hull surface, while others approximate the hull as an ellipsoid with proportions matching the ship's. 9218 Using this approximation, the ellipsoid's length corresponds to the ship's length while its width corresponds to the ship's breadth. 494 For longitudinal motion (that is, linear motion along an axis parallel to the ship's length), the added mass coefficient varies nearly linearly from 0.0 at a breadth-to-length ratio 9220of 0 (the ship is infinitely thin) up to 0.5 at a breadth-to-length ratio of 1 (a sphere).

498When the added mass coefficient is expressed as a percentage of the ship's mass, virtual mass can be calculated as 498mv498 = 498m498 (1 + 498xa498), where 498m498 is mass, and 498xa9223 is the added mass coefficient—for example, 0.2 for 20%. 9224 For typical displacement ship proportions, the longitudinal added mass ranges from about 4% to 15% of the mass of the ship. 9225 Conservative estimates generally use 20%.

556Propulsion

558There are a 9285variety of methods to propel a boat through the water. 9286 The oldest is by way of sails using the wind. 9287 The physics of sailing could fill several chapters on its own, so we won't go into detail here. 563 If you are interested in that topic, 563The Physics of Sailing Explained563 (Sheridan House), by 9289Bryon Anderson, is a good starting point. 9290 You can also check out the airplane chapter in this book and note that a sail is just a vertical wing. 9291 We will say this: if you choose to put a sailing vessel in your simulation, make sure to remember the golden rule! 9292 You cannot generally sail within 45 degrees of the direction the wind is coming from.

9295There are many different kinds of propulsion: rudders, shafted fixed-pitch propellers, shafted CPP propellers, azimuthing thrusters, propellers in nozzles, water jets, pump jets, contra-rotating, and the very odd Voith Schneider type. 9296 We can't get into all of them here, but the aforementioned list should get you started if you are interesting in modeling the specifics of different propulsor types.

574The major propulsion relationship for your simulation would be 575the thrust-to-propeller RPM or 9302thrust-to-throttle curve. 9303 The fly in the ointment here is that thrust is also dependent on forward speed. 9304 As the boat moves faster and faster through the water, the inflow velocity of the water into the propeller is higher and higher. 9305 This means that the difference between the velocity of the intake and the velocity of the output is smaller, reducing the amount of thrust. 581 In general, the thrust curve versus boat speed will look like 582Figure 16-69308 .

591An important physical phenomenon concerning propulsion that you may want to incorporate592 is 592cavitation9318 . 9319 Cavitation occurs when a propeller is moving fast enough that the low-pressure side of the blade starts spontaneously creating vapor bubbles. 9320 These bubbles exist for a short while, and then as the propeller turns, the static pressure changes. 9321 This higher static pressure causes the bubbles to collapse violently. 9322 This collapse is so fast and furious that it can cause metal erosion at a high rate. 9323 It will eat away at a propeller until it is no longer producing thrust. 9324 It is also very noisy. 9325 That is why submarine propellers are shaped very differently than other ships' propellers. 9326 They seek to limit cavitations so they're not heard by enemy vessels. 9327 The damage caused by cavitation also creates a speed limit on RPMs for a propeller. 9328 Cavitation is a real-life phenomenon you can exploit to penalize the player for driving around at high speed all the time.

606Maneuverability

608Another aspect of 9335ships and boats that is often oversimplified is maneuverability. 9336 Maneuverability is also a very complex topic whose numerical simulation is beyond the current realm of real-time simulation. 9337 However, with some simplifications and assumptions, it can be more accurately modeled than if you do not know the underlying framework. 9338 Almost all vessels maneuver by way of two methods: rudders or thrust vectoring. 9339 The users generally won't care about the differences, so you can model both by angling the thrust vector off-center.

617Rudders and Thrust Vectoring

619Although 9346rudders and thrust vectoring have the same result, there are some important differences. 9347 A thrust vectoring system, like a jet boat, can steer only when the vessel is producing thrust. 9348 A rudder, on the other hand, works only when the vessel has forward speed. 9349 If the boat isn't moving forward with enough speed, then the rudder can't produce a turning moment.

9352If you keep in mind those differences, you can model both systems the same way. 9353 The most important thing to keep in mind when modeling larger ships in your games is that they take significant time to respond to control inputs. 629 630Figure 16-7632 tracks the heading of a ship over time during what is called the 63210/10 maneuver9357 .

638Figure 16-7. 10/10 zig-zag test

9365A vessel is moving in a straight line, and the rudder is put over 10 degrees in one direction. 9366 Once the vessel's heading changes 10 degrees, the rudder is moved to the opposite side at the same angle. 9367 The initial turning time is the time it took for the vessel to change its heading 10 degrees. 9368 As large ships have enormous momentum, they will continue to turn even though the rudder is in the opposite direction. 644 The maximum deviation from the original heading minus the 10 degrees at which the rudder was flipped is called645 the 645overshoot angle9371 . 9372 The size of this angle is one measure of how slow the vessel is to respond to the helm. 9373 For larger ships, this can be between 15 degrees when light and 45 degrees when loaded with cargo.

9376The time to check yaw is the time in seconds it takes for the overshoot angle to be achieved and the vessel to start changing its heading again. 9377 This is repeated for the other side to detect any bias a vessel may have for turning in a particular direction. 9378 The moral of the story is that for anything other than a high-speed small craft, boats and ships can take a significant amount of time to respond to the helm. 654 Your simulation should strive to reproduce a turning ability that matches 655Figure 16-79381 for extra realism.

658A special kind of thrust vectoring is 660called 660throttle steering9385 . 9386 Imagine that a boat has two engines. 9387 If one is run in forward and the other in reverse, the vessel will turn quite rapidly. 9388 For a twin-engine vessel operating in close quarters, the rudders are often centered and the vessel maneuvered solely by altering the throttle settings of the two props.

666Propeller walk

668Another interesting 669maneuvering phenomenon that is closely related to thrust vectoring is called 669propeller walk669, or 669prop walk9395 . 9396 This is especially important for vessels with only one propeller moving in tight spaces. 9397 The cause of propeller walk is related to the fact that most propellers are installed at an angle to the horizon. 9398 This angle causes the thrust to be greater when the blades are moving down than when the blades are moving upward. 9399 In a propeller that turns clockwise, this creates a push to the right.

9402In forward gear the rudder is very effective at countering the propeller walk, but in reverse the rudder is much less effective, making the propeller walk much more noticeable. 678 This can add a significant amount of realism when you are simulating vessels in docking 9404maneuvers.
