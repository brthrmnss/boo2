1Chapter 17. Cars and Hovercraft

2What cars and 9417hovercraft have in common is that they operate in an essentially 2D manner. 9418 Unless they have jumped a ramp, both vehicles remain on the ground or water plane. 9419 In this chapter we'll discuss the forces behind each vehicle's method of travel and discuss how to accurately model them in your simulations.

8Cars

10In the 9425following sections we want to discuss certain aspects of the physics behind automobile motion. 9426 Like the previous four chapters, the purpose of this chapter is to explain, by example, certain physical phenomena. 9427 We also want to give you a basic understanding of the mechanics involved in automobile motion in case you want to simulate one in your games. 9428 In keeping with the theme of this book, we'll be talking about mechanics in the sense of rigid-body motion, and not in the sense of how an internal combustion engine works, or how power is transferred through the transmission system to the wheels, etc. 9429 Those are all internal to the car as a rigid body, and we'll focus on the external forces. 9430 We will, however, discuss how the torque applied to the drive wheel is translated to a force that pushes the car along.

61Power

63Now that you know 9481how to calculate the total resistance on your car, you can easily calculate the power required to overcome the resistance at a given speed. 68 Power is a measure of the amount of 68work9482 done by a force, or torque, over time. 9483 Mechanical work done by a force is equal to the force times the distance an object moves under the action of that force. 9484 It's expressed in units such as foot-pounds. 9485 Since power is a measure of work done over time, its units are, for example, foot-pounds per second. 72 Usually power in the context of car engine output is expressed in units of 72horsepower9486 , where 1 horsepower equals 550 ft-lbs/s.

92Stopping Distance

94Under normal 9511conditions, stopping distance is a function of the braking system and how hard the driver applies the brakes: the harder the brakes are applied, the shorter the stopping distance. 9512 That's not the case when the tires start to skid. 99 Under skidding conditions, stopping distance is a function of the 9514frictional force that develops between the tires and the road, in addition to the inclination of the roadway. 9515 If the car is traveling uphill, the skidding distance will be shorter because gravity helps slow the car, while it will tend to accelerate the car and increase the skidding distance when the car is traveling downhill.

104There's a simple formula that considers these factors that you can use to calculate skidding distance:

104d104s104 = V1042104 / [2g ( µ cos φ + sin φ)]

106Here 106d106s106 is the 108skidding distance, 108g108 the acceleration due to gravity, µ the coefficient of friction between the tires and road, 108V9522 the initial speed of the car, and φ the inclination of the roadway (where a positive angle means uphill and a negative angle means downhill). 9523 Note that this equation does not take into account any aerodynamic drag that will help slow the car down.

9526The coefficient of friction will vary depending on the condition of the tires and surface of the road, but for rubber on pavement the dynamic friction coefficient is typically around 0.4, while the static coefficient is around 0.55.

121Steering

122When you turn the 9538steering wheel of a car, the front wheels exert a side force such that the car starts to turn. 125 In terms of 9540Euler angles, this would be yaw, although Euler angles aren't usually used in discussions about turning cars. 9541 Even if the car's speed is constant, it experiences acceleration due to the fact that its velocity vector has changed direction. 9542 Remember, acceleration is the time rate of change in velocity, which has both magnitude and direction.

131For a car to maintain its curved path, there must 133be a 133centripetal9547 force ("center seeking" 9548 in Greek) that acts on the car. 135 When riding in a turning car, you feel an 138apparent 138centrifugal9552 acceleration, or force directed away from the center of the turn. 139 This acceleration is really a result of 139inertia9553 , the tendency of your body and the car to continue on their original path, and is not a real force acting on the car or your body. 9554 The real force is the centripetal force, and without it your car would continue on its straight path and not along the curve.

9557One of the most important aspects of racing is taking turns as fast as possible but without losing control. 9558 The longer you can wait to decelerate for the turn, and the sooner you can start accelerating again, the higher your average speed will be. 9559 It is interesting to note that when people ride in racecars, what really surprises them isn't the acceleration but the massive decelerations they can create through braking forces. 9560 A Formula One car regularly experiences decelerations of 4g, with 5–6g being the extreme value for certain race courses. 9561 Most road-legal sports cars can achieve about 1g of braking force. 9562 This allows the racecars to maintain speed until just entering the corner.

9574If this speed is exceeded, the cornering force will exceed the static coefficient of friction and the tires will begin to slide. 161 It may be tempting to replace the normal force, 161N9575 , with the weight of the vehicle (mass times gravity) and cancel out the mass of the car, but the normal force may not always be simplified as such, as we'll discuss in a moment. 9576 Also, in real life, the weight of a vehicle is rarely evenly distributed among all four tires and is definitely not when the car is acceleration or decelerating. 9577 In general, acceleration causes a weight shift to the aft tires, and deceleration causes weight to shift to the forward tires. 9578 This is important because, depending on where the weight is, the front or back tires will tend to have less available friction. 165 If one or the other sets of tires begins to slide, the car will either 167understeer or oversteer (see 168Figure 17-19583 ).

9591If the front wheels slide, the car understeers and the arc is larger than the driver intended. 9592 This is commonly caused by traveling too fast through a corner and trying to take the corner too tightly. 9593 However, if the driver breaks hard or even just lets off the gas, there will be a weight shift forward. 9594 This will keep the forward wheels from slipping but if too aggressive will cause the rear wheels to slip as weight is transferred away from them. 9595 This causes the car to turn more than the driver intended, and can even result in a spin. 9596 These two conditions limit the speed at which a car can complete a turn and also the amount of deceleration the car can handle once the turn is initiated.

185To increase the limit speed, 185v185limit9599 , we must increase the normal force. 9600 We could do so by increasing the car's mass, but this would have negative effects on the car's ability to accelerate or decelerate. 187 A better solution is the use of aerodynamic features to create what is called 187downforce9601 . 9602 You may have seen racecars or even street cars with large wing-like features called spoilers on their trunks. 9603 Formula 1 cars also have wing-like appendages on the front of the car. 9604 These are like the wings of an airplane but inverted so that instead of pulling the vehicle up, they actually push the vehicle down. 9605 These wings, therefore, increase the normal force, and their effects are proportional to speed so that more downforce is available at higher speeds...just when you need it. 9606 In fact, some very fast cars create so much downforce that if the road were inverted, the car would remain glued to the pavement and be able to drive upside-down.

9609The trade-off for this increased cornering limit speed is increased drag caused by the airfoils. 9610 This limits the top speed of the vehicle in areas of road that have no corners. 9611 It is good practice to allow users to select an angle of attack for their vehicle's airfoils. 9612 This forces them to choose between the option of higher top speed with slower cornering or faster cornering with lower top speed.

229Hovercraft

231Hovercraft, or air cushion vehicles (ACVs), 9648have made their way into a video game or two recently. 9649 Their appeal seems to stem from their futuristic aura, high speed, and levitating ability, which lets them go anywhere. 9650 In real life, hovercraft have been around since the 1950s and have been used in combat, search and rescue, cargo transport, ferrying, and recreational roles. 9651 They come in all shapes and sizes, but they all pretty much work the same, with the basic idea of getting the craft off the land or water to reduce its drag. 238 In 239Chapter 99654 , we touched on what forces you have to model when considering hovercraft, and now we'll talk about them in more detail.

242How Hovercraft Work

243The first hovercraft designs pumped air through an annular nozzle around the periphery of the craft (see 244Figure 17-39659 ). 9660 Large fans are used to feed the air through the nozzle under the craft. 9661 This jet of air creates a region of relatively high pressure over the area underneath the craft, which results in a net lifting force. 9662 The lifting force must equal the weight of the craft if it is to attain hovering flight. 249 This sort of lifting is known as 249aerostatic9663 lift. 250 The 252hover height is limited by the amount of 9667power available and the lifting fan's ability to pump enough air through the nozzle: the higher the hover height, the greater the power demand.

259Figure 17-3. Hovercraft configurations

9675This approach proved impractical because hover heights were very limited and made the clearance between the hard structure of the craft and the ground (or water) too small to overcome all but the smallest obstacles. 262 The solution to this problem was to fit a flexible 262skirt262 around the craft to contain the air cushion in what's called 263the 263plenum chamber263 (see 265Figure 17-39679 ). 9680 This approach extended the clearance between the ground and the hard structure of the craft significantly even though the gap between the bottom of the skirt and the ground was very small. 9681 This is the basic configuration of most hovercraft in operation today, although there are all sorts of skirt designs. 9682 Some of these skirts are simple curtains, while others are sophisticated pressurized bag and finger arrangements. 9683 The end result is that hovercraft fitted with skirts can clear relatively large obstacles without damage to their hard structure, and the skirt simply distorts and conforms to the terrain over which the craft operates.

9685The actual calculation of the aerostatic lift force is fairly complicated because the pressure distribution within the air cushion is nonuniform and because you must also take into account the performance of the lift fan system. 9686 There are theories available to treat both the annular jet and plenum chamber configurations, but they are beyond the scope of this book. 9687 Besides, for a game simulation, what's important is that you realize that the lift force must equal the weight of the craft in order for it to maintain equilibrium in hovering flight.

9689Ideally, the ability of a hovercraft to eliminate contact with the ground (or water) over which it operates means that it can travel relatively fast since it no longer experiences contact drag forces. 276 Notice we said 276ideally9690 . 9691 In reality, hovercraft often pitch and roll, causing parts of the skirt to drag, and any obstacle that comes into contact with the skirt will cause more drag. 9692 At any rate, while eliminating ground contact is good for speed, it's not so good for maneuverability.

280Hovercraft are 9696notoriously difficult to control since they glide across the ground. 9697 They tend to continue on their original trajectory even after you try to turn them. 9698 Currently, there are several means employed in various configurations for directional control. 9699 Some hovercraft use vertical tail rudders much like an airplane, while others actually vector their propulsion thrust. 9700 Still others use bow thrusters, which offer very good control. 9701 All of these means are fairly easy to model in a simulation; they are all simply forces acting on the craft at some distance from its center of gravity so as to create a yawing moment. 288 The 2D simulation that we walked you through in 289Chapter 99704 shows how to handle bow thrusters. 291 You can handle vertical tail rudders as we showed you 292in 294Chapter 159708 .

304When a hovercraft is operating over smooth land, the total drag acting against the hovercraft is 9720aerodynamic in nature. 9721 This assumes that drag induced by dragging the skirt or hitting obstacles is ignored. 308 The three components of aerodynamic drag are:

  • 311Skin friction and 312viscous pressure drag on the body of the craft

  • 315Induced drag316 when the craft is pitched

  • 319Momentum drag

321In equation form, the total drag is as follows:

321R321total321 = R321viscous321 + R321induced321 + R321momentum

323The first of these components, the viscous drag324 on the body of the craft, is the same sort of drag experienced by projectiles flying through the air, as explained in 326Chapter 69740 . 327 This drag is estimated using the by-now-familiar formula:

327R327viscous327 = (1/2) ρ V3272327 S327p327 C327d

328Here ρ is the mass density of air, 328V328 the speed of the hovercraft, 328S328p328 the projected frontal area of the craft normal to the direction of 328V328, and 328C328d9742 the drag coefficient. 329 330Typical values of 330C330d9744 for craft in operation today range from 0.25 to 0.4.

9747The next drag component, the induced drag, is a result of the craft assuming a pitched attitude when moving. 334 When the bow of the craft pitches up by an angle τ, there will be a component of the aerostatic lift vector that acts in a direction opposing 334V9748 . 335 This component is approximately equal to the weight of the craft times the tangent of the pitch angle:

335R335induced335 ≈ W (tan τ)

337Finally, momentum drag9752 results from the destruction of horizontal momentum of air, relative to the craft entering the lift fan intake. 339 This component is difficult to compute unless you know the properties of the entire lifting system such that the 9754mass flow rate of air into the fan is known. 341 Given the mass flow rate, 341R341momentum341 is equal to the mass flow rate times the velocity of the craft:

341R341momentum341 = (dm341fan341/dt) V

343Mass flow rate is expressed in units such as kg/s, which when multiplied by velocity in m/s yields 343N9757 .

346In 347Chapter 99762 we mentioned that it is beneficial to have the center of this drag be behind the center of gravity. 9763 This gives directional stability, as the vessel will tend to try to point into the apparent wind. 9764 Consider if the vessel yaws some angle, the forward velocity is creating a wind that is now hitting the side of the hovercraft. 9765 If the center of effort of this force is forward of the center of gravity, it will want to yaw the vessel more, increasing the side force and causing even greater drag until the vessel spins 180 degrees! 9766 If the center of effort of the wind is aft of the center of gravity, the vessel will spin back into the wind. 9767 You don't want the center of effort so far aft that it is too difficult to turn the vessel, but it is generally better to have the vessel naturally straighten out than to require constant steering input to maintain a steady course. 354 This is also true of sailboats and is called 354weather helm9768 . 355 9771This also occurs in airplanes. 9772 The solution in airplanes is similar to that of hovercraft: to fit tail fins that move the center of the area aft, increasing its directional stability. 9773 If you choose to model damage in your simulation, the loss of a tail fin will cause the vessel to be very difficult to control.

9776In addition to these three drag components, hovercraft will experience other forms of resistance when operating over water. 363 These additional components 365are 365wave365 drag and 365wetted9779 drag. 366 The equation for total drag can thus be revised for operation over water as follows:

366R366total366 = R366viscous366 + R366induced366 + R366momentum366 + R366wave366 + R366wetted

368When a hovercraft operates over water, its air cushion creates a depression in the water surface due to cushion pressure (see 369Figure 17-49784 ). 9785 At zero to low speeds, the weight of this displaced volume of water is equal to the weight of the craft, just as if the craft were floating in the water supported by buoyancy. 9786 As the craft starts to move forward, it tends to pitch up by the bow. 9787 When that happens, the surface of the water in the depressed region is approximately parallel to the bottom of the craft. 9788 As speed increases, the depression is reduced and the pitch angle tends to decrease.

401The so-called wetted drag is a function of several things:

  • 403The fact that parts of the hull and skirt tend to hit the water surface during flight

  • 405The impact of spray on the hull and skirt

  • 407The increase in weight as the hovercraft gets wet and sometimes takes on water

419Steering

421In 422Chapter 9422, the 9839hovercraft was steered using a bow thruster that pushed transversely forward of the center of gravity. 9840 In reality, most hovercraft are steered by vectoring the thrust of the propulsion fan via rudders attached directly aft of the fans. 9841 This can be modeled by angling the propulsive thrust.

9844The most important characteristic to remember about steering hovercraft is that they will not turn like a car or boat. 9845 The hovercraft, because it has lower friction with its environment, will take longer to turn and tends to continue in the direction it was heading while rotating. 9846 Once rotated, the thrust acts along a new vector. 9847 One possible maneuver in a hovercraft is to quickly rotate the vessel and then shut down the propulsive thrust. 9848 This would allow you to travel in one direction and point in another for as long as your momentum carries you. 9849 This could be very useful in strafing enemies or just racking up style points.
