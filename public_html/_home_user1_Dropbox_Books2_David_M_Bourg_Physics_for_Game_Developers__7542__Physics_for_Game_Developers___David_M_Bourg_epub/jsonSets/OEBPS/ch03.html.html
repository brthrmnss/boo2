11Forces

12As we mentioned in 13Chapter 213, you need to 2479understand the concept of force before you can fully understand the subject of kinetics. 2480 Kinematics is only half the battle. 2481 You are already familiar with the concept of force from your daily experiences. 2482 You exert a force on this book as you hold it in your hands, counteracting gravity. 2483 You exert force on your mouse as you move it from one point to another. 2484 When you play soccer, you exert force on the ball as you kick it. 2485 In general, force is what makes an object move, or more precisely, what produces an acceleration that changes the velocity. 2486 Even as you hold this book, although it may not be moving, you've effectively produced an acceleration that cancels the acceleration from gravity. 2487 When you kick that soccer ball, you change its velocity from, say, 0 when the ball is at rest to some positive value as the ball leaves your foot. 24 These are some examples of externally 25applied 25contact2490 forces.

29There's another broad category of forces, in addition to contact forces, 30called 30field30 forces or 32sometimes 32forces at a distance2496 . 2497 These forces can act on a body without actually having to make contact with it. 2498 A good example is the gravitational attraction between objects. 2499 Another example is the electromagnetic attraction between charged particles. 2500 The concept of a force field was developed long ago to help us visualize the interaction between objects subject to forces at a distance. 2501 You can say that an object is subjected to the gravitational field of another object. 2502 Thinking in terms of force fields can help you grasp the fact that an object can exert a force on another object without having to physically touch it.

2505Within these two broad categories of forces, there are specific types of forces related to various physical phenomena—forces due to friction, buoyancy, and pressure, among others. 2506 We'll discuss idealizations of several of these types of forces in this chapter. 2507 Later in this book, we'll revisit these forces from a more practical point of view.

46Before going further, we need to explain the implications of Newton's 47third law as introduced in 49Chapter 12513 . 2514 Remember, Newton's third law states that for every force acting on a body, there is an equal and opposite reacting force. 2515 This means that forces must exist in pairs—a single force can't exist by itself.

2517Consider the gravitational attraction between the earth and yourself. 2518 The earth is exerting a force—your weight—on you, accelerating you toward its center. 2519 Likewise, you are exerting a force on the earth, accelerating it toward you. 2520 The huge difference between your mass and the earth's makes the acceleration of the earth in this case so small that it's negligible. 2521 Earlier we said you are exerting a force on this book to hold it up; likewise, this book is exerting a force on your hands equal in magnitude but opposite in direction to the force you are exerting on it. 2522 You feel this reaction force as the book's weight.

2524This phenomenon of action-reaction is the basis for rocket propulsion. 2525 A rocket engine exerts force on the fuel molecules that are accelerated out of the engine exhaust nozzle. 62 The force required to accelerate these molecules is exerted back against the rocket as a reaction force 63called 63thrust2528 . 2529 Throughout the remainder of this book, you'll see many other examples of action-reaction, which is an important phenomenon in rigid-body dynamics. 2530 It is especially important when we are dealing with collisions and objects in contact, as you'll see later.

103Friction

105Frictional forces (friction) 2570always resist motion and are due to the interaction between contacting surfaces. 107 Thus, friction is a 2572contact force. 2573 Friction is always parallel to the contacting surfaces at the point of contact—that is, friction is tangential to the contacting surfaces. 2574 The magnitude of the frictional force is a function of the normal force between the contacting surfaces and the surface roughness.

199Fluid Dynamic Drag

201Fluid dynamic 2668drag forces oppose motion like friction. 2669 In fact, a major component of fluid dynamic drag is friction that results from the relative flow of the fluid over (and in contact with) the body's surface. 2670 Friction is not the only component of fluid dynamic drag, though. 2671 Depending on the shape of the body, its speed, and the nature of the fluid, fluid dynamic drag will have additional components due to pressure variations in the fluid as it flows around the body. 2672 If the body is located at the interface between two fluids (like a ship on the ocean where the two fluids are air and water), an additional component of drag will exist due to the wave generation.

2675In general, fluid dynamic drag is a complicated phenomenon that is a function of several factors. 2676 We won't go into detail in this section on all these factors, since we'll revisit this subject later. 213 However, we do want to discuss how 214the 214viscous2679 (frictional) component of these drag forces is typically idealized.

218Ideal viscous drag is a function of velocity and some experimentally 220determined 220drag coefficient2684 that's supposed to take into account the surface conditions of the body, the fluid properties (density and viscosity), and the flow conditions. 221 You'll typically see a formula for viscous drag force in the form:

221F221v221 = –C221f221 v

223where 223C223f223 is the drag coefficient, 223v2687 is the body's speed, and the minus sign means that the force opposes motion. 2688 This formula is valid for slow-moving objects in a viscous fluid. 2689 "Slow moving" 226 implies that the 228flow around the body is 228laminar2692 , which means that the flow streamlines are undisturbed and parallel.

231For fast-moving objects, you'll use the formula for 231F231v231 written as a function of speed squared as follows:

231F231v231 = –C231f231 v2312

2697 "Fast moving" 234 implies that the flow around the 236object is 236turbulent2700 , which means that the flow streamlines are no longer parallel and there is a sort of mixing effect in the flow around the object. 237 Note that the values of 237C237f2701 are generally not the same for these two equations. 238 In addition to the factors mentioned earlier, 238C238f2702 depends significantly on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent.

2705Both of these equations are very simplified and inadequate for practical analysis of fluid flow problems. 2706 However, they do offer certain advantages in computer game simulations. 2707 Most obviously, these formulas are easy to implement—you need only know the velocity of the body under consideration, which you get from your kinematic equations, and an assumed value for the drag coefficient. 2708 This is convenient, as your game world will typically have many different types of objects of all sizes and shapes that would make rigorous analysis of each of their drag properties impractical. 2709 If the illusion of realism is all you need, not real-life accuracy, then these formulas may be sufficient.

2712Another advantage of using these idealized formulas is that you can tweak the drag coefficients as you see fit to help reduce numerical instabilities when solving the equations of motion, while maintaining the illusion of realistic behavior. 2713 If real-life accuracy is what you're going for, then you'll have no choice but to consider a more involved (read: complicated) approach for determining fluid dynamic drag. 250 We'll talk more about drag in 251Chapter 6251 through 253Chapter 102717 .

255Pressure

256Many people confuse 2722pressure with force. 2723 You have probably heard people say, when explaining a phenomenon, something like, 2724 "It pushed with a force of 100 pounds per square inch." 2725 While you understand what They mean, They are technically referring to pressure, not force. 262 Pressure is force per unit area, thus the 265units 265pounds per square inch265 (psi) or 265pounds per square foot265 (psf) 2731and so on. 2732 Given the pressure, you'll need to know the total area acted on by this pressure in order to determine the resultant force. 269 Force equals pressure times area:

269F = PA

2735This formula tells you that for constant pressure, the greater the area acted upon, the greater the resultant force. 2736 If you rearrange this equation solving for pressure, you'll see that pressure is inversely proportional to area—that is, the greater the area for a given applied force, the smaller the resulting pressure and vice versa.

273P = F/A

2739An important characteristic of pressure is that it always acts normally (perpendicularly) to the surface of the body or object it is acting on. 2740 This fact gives you a clue as to the direction of the resultant force vector.

2743We wanted to mention pressure here because you'll be working with it to calculate forces when you get to the chapters in this book that cover the mechanics of ships, boats, and hovercraft. 280 There, the pressures that you'll consider are 2746hydrostatic pressure (buoyancy) and aerostatic lift. 2747 We'll take a brief look at buoyancy next.

286Buoyancy

288You've no doubt felt the 2754effects of buoyancy when immersing yourself in the bathtub. 2755 Buoyancy is why you feel lighter in water than you do in air and why some people can float on their backs in a swimming pool.

2758Buoyancy is a force that develops when an object is immersed in a fluid. 2759 It's a function of the volume of the object and the density of the fluid and results from the pressure differential between the fluid just above the object and the fluid just below the object. 296 Pressure 2762increases the deeper you go in a fluid, thus the pressure is greater at the bottom of an object of a given height than it is at the top of the object. 299 Consider the cube shown in 300Figure 3-22765 .

413Force and Torque

414We need to make the 2880distinction here between force and torque.417[41812418] Force is what causes 425linear acceleration, while 2890torque is what causes rotational acceleration. 2891 Torque is force times distance. 428 Specifically, to calculate the 2893torque applied by a force acting on an object, you need to calculate the perpendicular distance from the axis of rotation to the line of action of the force and then multiply this distance by the magnitude of the force.

2896This calculation gives the magnitude of the torque. 2897 Typical units for force are pounds, newtons, and tons. 2898 Since torque is force times a distance, its units take the form of a length unit times a force unit (e.g., foot-pounds, newton-meters, or foot-tons).

2901Since both force and torque are vector quantities, you must also determine the direction of the torque vector. 2902 The force vector is easy to visualize: its line of action passes through the point of application of the force, with its direction determined by the direction in which the force is applied. 439 As a vector, the torque's line of action is along the axis of rotation, with the direction determined by the 440direction of rotation and 442the 442right hand rule442 (see 443Figure 3-32908 ). 445 As noted in 446Chapter 22911 , the right hand rule is a simple trick to help you keep track of vector directions—in this case, the torque vector. 2912 Pretend to curl the fingers of your right hand around the axis of rotation with your fingertips pointing in the direction of rotation. 2913 Now extend your thumb, as though you are giving a thumbs up, while keeping your fingers curled around the axis. 2914 The direction that your thumb is pointing indicates the direction of the torque vector. 451 Note that this makes the torque vector perpendicular to the applied force vector, as shown in 452Figure 3-32917 .

505When you are considering rigid bodies that are not physically constrained to rotate about a fixed axis, any force acting through 2970the body's center of mass will not produce a torque on the body about its center of gravity. 507 In this case, the axis of rotation passes through the center of mass of the body and the vector 507r2971 would be 0 (all components 0). 2972 When a force acts through a point on the body some distance away from its center of mass, a torque on the body will develop, and the angular motion of the body will be affected. 509 Generally, 2975field forces, which are forces at a distance, are assumed to act through a body's center of mass; thus, only the body's linear motion will be affected unless the body is constrained to rotate about a fixed point. 512 Other contact forces, however, generally do not act through a body's center of mass (they could but aren't necessarily assumed to) and tend to affect the body's angular motion as well as its 2978linear motion.
