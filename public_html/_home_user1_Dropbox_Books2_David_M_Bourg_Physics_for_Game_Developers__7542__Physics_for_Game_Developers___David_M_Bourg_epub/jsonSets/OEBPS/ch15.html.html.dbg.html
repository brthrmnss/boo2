145Lift and Drag

147When an airfoil 8194moves through a fluid such as air, lift is produced. 152 The mechanisms by which this occurs are similar to those in the case of the Magnus lift force, discussed earlier in 153Chapter 68197 , in that Bernoulli's law is still in effect. 155 However, this time, instead of rotation it's the airfoil's shape and 8200angle of attack that affect the flow of air so as to create lift.

160Figure 15-5160 shows an airfoil section moving through air at a speed 160V8203 . 161 161V8204 is the relative velocity between the foil and the undisturbed air ahead of the foil. 8205 As the air hits and moves around the foil, it splits at the forward stagnation point located near the foil leading edge such that air flows both over and under the foil. 8206 The air that flows under the foil gets deflected downward, while the air that flows over the foil speeds up as it goes around the leading edge and over the surface of the foil. 164 The air then flows smoothly off the trailing edge; this is the so-called 8208Kutta condition. 8209 Ideally, the boundary layer remains 8210 "attached" 168 to the foil without separating as in the case of the sphere discussed in 169Chapter 68213 .

176Figure 15-5. Airfoil moving through air

178The relatively fast-moving air above the foil results in a region of low pressure above the foil (remember 8223Bernoulli's equation that shows pressure is inversely proportional to velocity in fluid flow). 8224 The air hitting and moving along the underside of the foil creates a region of relatively high pressure. 8225 The combined effect of this flow pattern is to create regions of relatively low and high pressure above and below the airfoil. 8226 It's this pressure differential that gives rise to the lift force. 8227 By definition, the lift force is perpendicular to the line of flight—that is, the velocity vector.

8230Note that the airfoil does not have to be cambered in order to generate lift; a flat plate oriented at an angle of attack relative to the airflow will also generate lift. 8231 Likewise, an airfoil does not have to have an angle of attack either. 8232 Cambered airfoils can generate lift at 0, or even negative, angles of attack. 8233 Thus, in general, the total lift force on an airfoil is composed of two components: the lift due to camber and the lift due to attack angle.

8236Theoretically, the thickness of an airfoil does not contribute to lift. 8237 You can, after all, have a thin curved wing as in the case of wings made from fabric (such as those used for hang gliders). 8238 In practice, thickness is utilized for structural reasons. 8239 Further, thickness at the leading edge can help delay stall (more on this in a moment).

8242The pressure differential between the upper and lower surfaces of the airfoil also gives rise to a drag force that acts in line with, but opposing, the velocity vector. 8243 The lift and drag forces are perpendicular to each other and lie in the plane defined by the velocity vector and the vector normal (perpendicular) to the airfoil chord line. 8244 When combined, these two force components, lift and drag, yield the resultant force acting on the airfoil in flight. 202 This is illustrated in 203Figure 15-58247 .

8249Both lift and drag are functions of air density, speed, viscosity, surface area, aspect ratio, and angle of attack. 207 Traditionally, the lift and drag properties of a given foil design are expressed in terms of nondimensional coefficients C207L207 and C207D207, respectively:

207C207L207 = L / [(1/2) ρ V2072207 S]
207C207D207 = D / [(1/2) ρ V2072207 S]

208where 208S208 is the wing planform area (span times chord for rectangular wings), 208L208 is the lift force, 208D208 is the drag force, 208V8251 is the speed through the air, and ρ (rho) is air density. 8252 These coefficients are experimentally determined from wind tunnel tests of model airfoil designs at various angles of attack. 8253 The results of these tests are usually presented as graphs of lift and drag coefficient versus attack angle. 211 212Figure 15-6212 through 213Figure 15-8213 illustrate some typical lift and drag charts for a wing 8259section.

8288In practice, the flow of air around a wing is not strictly two-dimensional—that is, flowing uniformly over each parallel cross section of the wing—and there exists a span-wise flow of air along the wing. 8289 The flow is said to be three-dimensional. 247 The more three-dimensional the flow, the 8291less efficient the wing.249[250258294 ] This effect is reduced on longer, high-aspect-ratio wings (and wings with end plates where the effective aspect ratio is increased); thus, high-aspect-ratio wings are comparatively more efficient.

8296To account for the effect of aspect ratio, wing sections of various aspect ratios for a given foil design are usually tested so as to produce a family of lift and drag curves versus attack angle. 254 There are other geometrical factors that affect the flow around wings; for a rigorous treatment of these, we refer you to 256the 256Theory of Wing Sections256 and 256Fluid-Dynamic Lift8299 .257[25826258]

260Turning back to 261Figure 15-68305 , you'll notice that the drag coefficient increases sharply with attack angle. 8306 This is reasonable, as you would expect the wing to produce the most drag when oriented flat against or perpendicular to the flow of air.

265A look at the lift coefficient curve, which initially increases linearly8309 with attack angle, shows that at some attack angle the lift coefficient reaches a maximum value. 267 This angle is 268called the 268critical attack angle8312 . 270 For angles beyond the critical, the lift coefficient drops off rapidly and the airfoil (or wing) 272will 272stall8315 and cease to produce lift. 8316 This is bad. 8317 When an aircraft stalls in the air, it will begin to drop rapidly until the pilot corrects the stall situation by, for example, reducing pitch and increasing thrust. 275 When stall occurs, the air no longer flows smoothly over the trailing edge, and the corresponding high angle of attack results in flow separation (as illustrated in 276Figure 15-98320 ). 8321 This loss in lift is also accompanied by an increase in drag.

8329Theoretically, the resultant force acting on an airfoil acts through a point located at one-quarter the chord length aft of the leading edge. 287 This is called the 288quarter-chord8332 point. 290 8334In reality, the resultant force line of action will vary depending on attack angle, pressure distribution, and speed, among other factors. 8335 However, in practice it is reasonable to assume that the line of action passes through the quarter-chord point for typical operational conditions. 8336 To account for the difference between the actual line of action of the resultant and the quarter-chord point, we must consider the pitching moment about the quarter-chord point. 8337 This pitching moment tends to tilt the leading edge of the foil down. 8338 In some cases this moment is relatively small compared to the other moments acting on the aircraft, and it may be neglected.296[297278341 ] An exception may be when the foil has deflected flaps.

313Other Forces

315The most notable force that we've yet to discuss is thrust—the 8359propulsion force. 8360 Thrust provides forward motion; without it, the aircraft's wings can't generate lift and the aircraft won't fly. 318 Thrust, whether generated by a propeller or a jet engine, is usually expressed in pounds, and a common ratio used to compare the relative merits of aircraft powering is 319the 319thrust-to-weight ratio8363 . 8364 This ratio is the maximum thrust deliverable by the propulsion plant divided by the aircraft's total weight. 8365 When the thrust-to-weight ratio is greater than one, the aircraft is capable of overcoming gravity in a vertical climb. 8366 This is more like a rocket than a traditional airplane. 8367 Most normal planes are not capable of this, but many military planes do have thrust-to-weight ratios of greater than one. 8368 However, airplane engines rely on oxygen in the atmosphere to combust their fuel with and to produce the force that propels them forward. 8369 As the plane climbs higher, the engines will have less oxygen and produce less thrust. 8370 The thrust-to-weight ratio will fall, and eventually the plane will again need lift from the wings to maintain its altitude. 8371 Even when the plane is climbing vertically like a rocket, the wings still generate lift, and in this case try to pull the airplane away from a vertical trajectory.

8374Besides gravity, thrust, wing lift, and wing drag, there are other forces that act on an aircraft in flight. 332 These are 8376drag forces (and lift in some cases) on the various components of the aircraft besides the wings. 8377 For example, the fuselage contributes to the overall drag acting on the aircraft. 8378 Additionally, anything sticking out of the fuselage will contribute to the overall drag. 336 If it's not a wing, anything sticking out of the fuselage is typically called an 336appendage8379 . 8380 Some examples of appendages are the aircraft landing gear, canopy, bombs, missiles, fuel pods, and air intakes.

340Typically, drag data for fuselages and appendages is expressed in terms of a 341drag coefficient similar to that discussed in 343Chapter 6343, where experimentally determined drag forces are nondimensionalized by projected frontal area (S), density (ρ), and velocity squared (V34328386 ). 344 This means that the experimentally measured drag force is divided by the quantity (1/2) ρ V34428387 S to get the dimensionless drag coefficient. 8388 Depending on the object under consideration, the drag coefficient data will be presented as a function of some important geometric parameter, such as attack angle in the case of airfoils, or length-to-height ratio in the case of canopies. 346 Here again, 347Hoerner's 347Fluid-Dynamic Drag8391 is an excellent source of practical data for all sorts of fuselage shapes and appendages.

8394For example, when an aircraft's landing gear is down, the wheels (as well as associated mechanical gear) contribute to the overall drag force on the aircraft. 8395 Hoerner reports drag coefficients based on the frontal area of some small-plane landing-gear designs to be in the range of 0.25 to 0.55. 8396 By comparison, drag coefficients for typical external storage pods (such as for fuel), which are usually streamlined, can range from 0.06 to 0.26.

356Another component of the total drag force acting on aircraft in flight is due to 8401skin friction. 8402 Aircraft wings, fuselages, and appendages are not completely smooth. 8403 Weld seams, rivets, and even paint cause surface imperfections that increase frictional drag. 361 As in the case of the sphere data presented in 362Chapter 68406 . 8407 This frictional drag is dependent on the nature of the flow around the part of the aircraft under consideration—that is, whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. 8408 This implies that frictional drag coefficients for specific surfaces will generally be a function of the Reynolds number.

8410In a rigorous analysis of a specific aircraft's flight, you'd of course want to consider all these additional drag components. 368 If you're interested in seeing the nitty-gritty details of such calculations, we suggest you take a look at 369Chapter 14369 of 369Fluid-Dynamic Drag369, where Hoerner gives a detailed example calculation of the total drag force on a fighter 8415aircraft.

375Control

377The flaps located on 8421the inboard trailing edge of the wing in our model are used to alter the chord and camber of the wing section to increase lift at a given speed. 379 8423Flaps are used primarily to increase lift during slow speed flight, such as when taking off or landing. 8424 When landing, flaps are typically deployed at a high downward angle (downward flap deflections are considered positive) on the order to 30° to 60°. 8425 This increases both the lift and drag of the wings. 8426 During landing, this increase in drag also assists in slowing the aircraft to a suitable landing speed. 8427 During takeoff, this increase in drag works against you in that it necessitates higher thrust to get up to speed; thus, flaps may not be deployed to as great an angle as when you are landing.

387Ailerons control or 8431induce roll motion by producing differential lift between the port and starboard wing sections. 8432 The basic aileron is nothing more than a pair of trailing-edge flaps fitted to the tips of the wings. 8433 These flaps move opposite each other, one deflecting upward and the other downward, to create a lift differential between the port and starboard wings. 8434 This lift differential, separated by the distance between the ailerons, creates a torque that rolls the aircraft. 8435 To roll the aircraft to the port side (the pilot's left), the starboard aileron would be deflected in a downward direction while the port aileron would be deflected in an upward direction relative to the pilot. 8436 Likewise, the opposite deflections of the ailerons would induce a roll to the starboard side. 8437 In a real aircraft, the pilot controls the ailerons by moving the flight stick to either the left or right.

397Elevators, 8441the tail 8442 "wings," 8443 are used to control the pitch of the aircraft. 401 (Elevators can be flaps, as shown in 402Figure 15-28446 , or the entire tail wing can rotate as on the Lockheed Martin F 16.) When the Elevators are deflected such that their trailing edge goes down with respect to the pilot, a nose down pitch rotation will be induced; that is, the tail of the aircraft will tend to rise relative to its nose, and the aircraft will dive. 8447 In an actual aircraft, the pilot achieves this by pushing the flight stick forward. 8448 When Elevators are deflected such that their tailing edge goes up, a nose up pitch rotation will be induced.

407Elevators are very important 408for 408trimming8452 (adjusting the pitch of) the aircraft. 8453 Generally, the aircraft's center of gravity is located above the mean quarter-chord line of the aircraft wings such that the center of gravity is in line with the main lift force. 411 However, as we explained earlier, the lift force does not always pass through the 8456quarter-chord point. 8457 Further, the aircraft's center of gravity may very well change during flight—for example, as fuel is burned off and when ordnance is released. 415 By controlling the elevators, the pilot is able to adjust the 415attitude8458 of the aircraft such that all of the forces balance and the aircraft flies at the desired orientation (pitch angle).

418Finally, the rudder is 8463used to control yaw. 8464 The pilot uses foot pedals to control the rudder; pushing the left (port) pedal yaws left and pushing the right pedals yaws right (starboard). 8465 The rudder is useful for fine-tuning the alignment of the aircraft for approach on landing or when sighting up a target. 8466 Typically, large rudder action tends to also induce roll motion that must be compensated for by proper use of the ailerons.

8469In some cases the rudder consists of a flap on the trailing edge of the vertical tail, while in other cases there is no rudder flap and the entire vertical tail rotates. 8470 In both cases, the vertical tail, which also provides directional stability, will usually have a symmetric airfoil shape; that is, its mean camber line will be coincident with its chord line. 8471 When the aircraft is flying straight and level, the tail will not generate lift since it is symmetric and its attack angle will be 0. 8472 However, if the plane sideslips (yaws relative to its flight direction), then the tail will be at an angle of attack and will generate lift, tending to push the plane back to its original orientation.

432Modeling

434Although we've yet to 436cover a lot of the material required to implement a real-time flight simulator, we'd like to go ahead and outline some of the steps necessary to calculate the lift and drag forces on your model aircraft:

  1. 8481Discretize the lifting surfaces into a number of smaller wing sections.

  2. 8484Collect geometric and foil performance data.

  3. 8487Calculate the relative air velocity over each wing section.

  4. 8490Calculate the attack angle for each wing section.

  5. 8493Determine the appropriate lift and drag coefficients and calculate lift and drag forces.

8496The first step is relatively straightforward in that you need to divide the aircraft into smaller sections where each section is approximately uniform in characteristics. 454 Performing this step for the model shown in 455Figure 15-28499 , you might divide the wing into four sections—one for each wing section that's fitted with an aileron and one for each section that's fitted with a flap. 8500 You could also use two sections to model the elevators, one port and one starboard, and another section to model the tail/rudder. 8501 Finally, you could lump the entire fuselage together as one additional section or further subdivide it into smaller sections depending on how detailed you want to get.

8503If you're going to model your aircraft as a rigid body, you'll have to account for all of the forces and moments acting on the aircraft while it is in flight. 8504 Since the aircraft is composed of a number of different components, each contributing to the total lift and drag, you'll have to break up your calculations into a number of smaller chunks and then sum all contributions to get the resultant lift and drag forces. 8505 You can then use these resultant forces along with thrust and gravity in the equations of motion for your aircraft. 8506 You can, of course, refine your model further by adding more components for such items as the cockpit canopy, landing gear, external fuel pods, bombs, etc. 8507 The level of detail to which you go depends on the degree of accuracy you're going for. 8508 If you are trying to mimic the flight performance of a specific aircraft, then you need to sharpen your pencil.

8510Once you've defined each section, you must now prepare the appropriate geometric and performance data. 8511 For example, for the wings and other lifting surfaces you'll need to determine each section's initial incidence angle (its fixed pitch or attack angle relative to the aircraft reference system), span, chord length, aspect ratio, planform area, and quarter-chord location relative to the aircraft's center of gravity. 8512 You'll also have to prepare a table of lift and drag coefficients versus attack angle appropriate for the section under consideration. 8513 Since this data is usually presented in graphical form, you'll have to pull data from the charts to build your lookup table for use in your game. 8514 Finally, you'll need to calculate the unit normal vector perpendicular to the plane of each wing section. 472 (You'll need this later when calculating angle of attack.)

8516These first two steps need only be performed once at the beginning of your game or simulation since the data will remain constant (unless your plane changes shape or its center of gravity shifts during your simulation).

8518The third step involves calculating the relative velocity between the air and each component so you can calculate lift and drag forces. 8519 At first glance, this might seem trivial since the aircraft will be traveling at an air speed that will be known to you during your simulation. 8520 However, you must also remember that the aircraft is a rigid body and in addition to the linear velocity of its center of gravity, you must account for its rotational velocity.

479Back in 480Chapter 2480, we gave you a formula to calculate the relative velocity of any point on a rigid body that was undergoing both linear and rotational motion:

480v480R480 = v480cg480 + (ω × r)

8525This is the formula you'll need to calculate the relative velocity at each component in your model. 483 In this case, 483v483cg483 is the vector representing the air speed and flight direction of the aircraft, ω (omega) is the angular velocity vector of the aircraft, and 483r8526 is the distance vector from the aircraft's center of gravity to the component under consideration.

8528When dealing with wings, once you have the relative velocity vector, you can proceed to calculate the attack angle for each wing section. 8529 The drag force vector will be parallel to the relative velocity vector, while the lift force vector will be perpendicular to the velocity vector. 8530 Angle of attack is then the angle between the lift force vector and the normal vector perpendicular to the plane of the wing section. 8531 This involves taking the dot product of these two vectors.

8533Once you have the attack angle, you can go to your coefficient of lift and drag versus attack angle tables to determine the lift and drag coefficients to use at this instant in your simulation. 491 With these coefficients, you can use the following formulas to estimate the magnitudes of lift and drag forces on the wing section under consideration:

491Lift = C491L491 (1/2) ρ V4912491 S
491Drag = C491D491 (1/2) ρ V4912491 S

8535This is a very simplified approach that only approximates the lift and drag characteristics. 8536 This approach does not account for span-wise flow effects, or the flow effects between adjacent wing sections. 8537 Nor does this approach account for air disturbances, such as downwash, that may affect the relative angle of attack for a wing section. 8538 Further, the airflow over each wing section is assumed to be steady and uniform.

     Element[7].fIncidence = 0.0f;

     Element[7].iFlap = 0;

     // This is required when you are calculating the relative air velocity for
     // lift and drag calculations.
     for (i = 0; i< 8; i++)
     {
          in = DegreesToRadians(Element[i].fIncidence);
          di = DegreesToRadians(Element[i].fDihedral);
          Element[i].vNormal = Vector((float)sin(in), (float)(cos(in)*sin(di)),
                                      (float)(cos(in)*cos(di)));
          Element[i].vNormal.Normalize();
     }

     mass = 0;
     for (i = 0; i< 8; i++)
          mass += Element[i].fMass;

     // Calculate combined center of gravity location
     vMoment = Vector(0.0f, 0.0f, 0.0f);
     for (i = 0; i< 8; i++)
     {
          vMoment += Element[i].fMass*Element[i].vDCoords;
     }
     CG = vMoment/mass;

     // Calculate coordinates of each element with respect to the combined CG
     for (i = 0; i< 8; i++)
     {
          Element[i].vCGCoords = Element[i].vDCoords − CG;
     }

     // combined elements.
     // (This inertia matrix (tensor) is in body coordinates)

     Ixy = 0;     Ixz = 0;     Iyz = 0;

                 (Element[i].vCGCoords.y*Element[i].vCGCoords.y +
                 Element[i].vCGCoords.z*Element[i].vCGCoords.z);
          Iyy += Element[i].vLocalInertia.y + Element[i].fMass *
                 (Element[i].vCGCoords.z*Element[i].vCGCoords.z +
                 Element[i].vCGCoords.x*Element[i].vCGCoords.x);
          Izz += Element[i].vLocalInertia.z + Element[i].fMass *
                 (Element[i].vCGCoords.x*Element[i].vCGCoords.x +
                 Element[i].vCGCoords.y*Element[i].vCGCoords.y);
          Ixy += Element[i].fMass * (Element[i].vCGCoords.x *
                 Element[i].vCGCoords.y);
          Ixz += Element[i].fMass * (Element[i].vCGCoords.x *
                 Element[i].vCGCoords.z);
          Iyz += Element[i].fMass * (Element[i].vCGCoords.y *
                 Element[i].vCGCoords.z);
     }

     // Finally, set up the airplane's mass and its inertia matrix and take the
     // inverse of the inertia matrix.
     Airplane.fMass = mass;
     Airplane.mInertia.e11 = Ixx;
     Airplane.mInertia.e12 = -Ixy;
     Airplane.mInertia.e13 = -Ixz;
     Airplane.mInertia.e21 = -Ixy;
     Airplane.mInertia.e22 = Iyy;
     Airplane.mInertia.e23 = -Iyz;
     Airplane.mInertia.e31 = -Ixz;
     Airplane.mInertia.e32 = -Iyz;
     Airplane.mInertia.e33 = Izz;

     Airplane.mInertiaInverse = Airplane.mInertia.Inverse();
}

8591The very first thing this function does is initialize the elements with the properties that we've defined to approximate the aircraft. 549 Each element is given a mass, a set of 549design coordinates549 to its center of mass, a set of moments of inertia about each element's center of mass, an initial incidence angle, a planform area, and 550a 550dihedral angle8594 .

8597The design coordinates are the coordinates of the element with respect to an origin located at the very tip of the aircraft's tail, on its centerline and at its baseline. 8598 The x-axis of this system points toward the nose of the aircraft, while the y-axis points toward the port side. 8599 The z-axis points up. 8600 You have to set up your elements in this design coordinate system first because you don't yet know the location of the whole aircraft's center of mass, which is the combined center of mass of all of the elements. 8601 Ultimately, you want each element referenced from the combined center of mass because it's the center of mass that you'll track during the simulation.

8604The dihedral angle is the angle about the x-axis at which the element is initially set. 8605 For our model, all of the elements have a 0 dihedral angle; that is, they are horizontal, except for the tail rudder, which has a 90° dihedral since it is oriented vertically.

8608After we've set up the elements, the first calculation that this function performs is to find the unit normal vector to each element's surface based on the element's incidence and dihedral angles. 8609 You need this direction vector to help calculate the angle of attack between the airflow and the element.

8612The next calculation is the total mass calculation, which is simply the sum of all element masses. 570 Immediately following that, we determine the combined center of gravity location using the technique we discussed in 571Chapter 18615 . 8616 The coordinates to the combined center of gravity are referenced to the design coordinate system. 8617 You need to subtract this coordinate from the design coordinate of each element in order to determine each element's coordinates relative to the combined center of gravity. 575 After that, you're all set with the exception of the combined moment of inertia tensor, which we already discussed in 576Chapter 11576 and 578Chapter 128621 .

//------------------------------------------------------------------------//
//  Given the attack angle and the status of the flaps, this function
//  returns the appropriate lift coefficient for a cambered airfoil with
//  a plain trailing-edge flap (+/- 15 degree deflection).
//------------------------------------------------------------------------//
float     LiftCoefficient(float angle, int flaps)
{
     float clf0[9] = {−0.54f, −0.2f, 0.2f, 0.57f, 0.92f, 1.21f, 1.43f, 1.4f,
                      1.0f};
     float clfd[9] = {0.0f, 0.45f, 0.85f, 1.02f, 1.39f, 1.65f, 1.75f, 1.38f,
                      1.17f};
     float clfu[9] = {−0.74f, −0.4f, 0.0f, 0.27f, 0.63f, 0.92f, 1.03f, 1.1f,
                      0.78f};
     float a[9]      = {−8.0f, −4.0f, 0.0f, 4.0f, 8.0f, 12.0f, 16.0f, 20.0f,
                        24.0f};
     float cl;
     int   i;

     cl = 0;
     for (i=0; i<8; i++)
     {
          if( (a[i] <= angle) && (a[i+1] > angle) )
          {
               switch(flaps)
               {
                    case 0:// flaps not deflected
                         cl = clf0[i] - (a[i] - angle) * (clf0[i] - clf0[i+1]) /
                             (a[i] - a[i+1]);
                         break;
                    case −1: // flaps down
                         cl = clfd[i] - (a[i] - angle) * (clfd[i] - clfd[i+1]) /
                             (a[i] - a[i+1]);
                         break;
                    case 1: // flaps up
                         cl = clfu[i] - (a[i] - angle) * (clfu[i] - clfu[i+1]) /
                             (a[i] - a[i+1]);
                         break;
               }
               break;
          }
     }

     return cl;
}

//------------------------------------------------------------------------//
// This function calculates all of the forces and moments acting on the
// plane at any given time.
//------------------------------------------------------------------------//
void     CalcAirplaneLoads(void)
{
     Vector     Fb, Mb;

     // reset forces and moments:
     Airplane.vForces.x = 0.0f;
     Airplane.vForces.y = 0.0f;
     Airplane.vForces.z = 0.0f;

     Airplane.vMoments.x = 0.0f;
     Airplane.vMoments.y = 0.0f;
     Airplane.vMoments.z = 0.0f;

     Fb.x = 0.0f;     Mb.x = 0.0f;
     Fb.y = 0.0f;     Mb.y = 0.0f;
     Fb.z = 0.0f;     Mb.z = 0.0f;

     // Define the thrust vector, which acts through the plane's CG
     Thrust.x = 1.0f;
     Thrust.y = 0.0f;
     Thrust.z = 0.0f;
     Thrust *= ThrustForce;

     // Calculate forces and moments in body space:
     Vector    vLocalVelocity;
     float     fLocalSpeed;
     Vector    vDragVector;
     Vector    vLiftVector;
     float     fAttackAngle;
     float     tmp;
     Vector    vResultant;
     int       i;
     Vector    vtmp;

     Stalling = false;

     for(i=0; i<7; i++) // loop through the seven lifting elements
                        // skipping the fuselage
     {
          if (i == 6) // The tail/rudder is a special case since it can rotate;
          {           // thus, you have to recalculate the normal vector.
               float in, di;
               in = DegreesToRadians(Element[i].fIncidence); // incidence angle
               di = DegreesToRadians(Element[i].fDihedral);  // dihedral angle
               Element[i].vNormal = Vector(     (float)sin(in),
                                                (float)(cos(in)*sin(di)),
                                                (float)(cos(in)*cos(di)));
               Element[i].vNormal.Normalize();
          }

          // Calculate local velocity at element
          // The local velocity includes the velocity due to linear
          // motion of the airplane,
          // plus the velocity at each element due to the
          // rotation of the airplane.

          // Here's the rotational part
          vtmp = Airplane.vAngularVelocity^Element[i].vCGCoords;

          vLocalVelocity = Airplane.vVelocityBody + vtmp;

          // Calculate local air speed
          fLocalSpeed = vLocalVelocity.Magnitude();

          // Find the direction in which drag will act.
          // Drag always acts inline with the relative
          // velocity but in the opposing direction
          if(fLocalSpeed > 1.)
               vDragVector = -vLocalVelocity/fLocalSpeed;

          // Find the direction in which lift will act.
          // Lift is always perpendicular to the drag vector
          vLiftVector = (vDragVector^Element[i].vNormal)^vDragVector;
          tmp = vLiftVector.Magnitude();
          vLiftVector.Normalize();

          // Find the angle of attack.
          // The attack angle is the angle between the lift vector and the
          // element normal vector. Note, the sine of the attack angle
          // is equal to the cosine of the angle between the drag vector and
          // the normal vector.
          tmp = vDragVector*Element[i].vNormal;
          if(tmp > 1.) tmp = 1;
          if(tmp < −1) tmp = −1;
          fAttackAngle = RadiansToDegrees((float) asin(tmp));

          // Determine the resultant force (lift and drag) on the element.
          tmp = 0.5f * rho * fLocalSpeed*fLocalSpeed * Element[i].fArea;
          if (i == 6) // Tail/rudder
          {
               vResultant =  (vLiftVector*RudderLiftCoefficient(fAttackAngle) +
                              vDragVector*RudderDragCoefficient(fAttackAngle))
                              * tmp;
          } else
               vResultant =  (vLiftVector*LiftCoefficient(fAttackAngle,
                              Element[i].iFlap) +
                              vDragVector*DragCoefficient(fAttackAngle,
                              Element[i].iFlap) ) * tmp;
          // Check for stall.
          // We can easily determine stall by noting when the coefficient
          // of lift is 0. In reality, stall warning devices give warnings well
          // before the lift goes to 0 to give the pilot time to correct.
          if (i<=0)
          {
               if (LiftCoefficient(fAttackAngle, Element[i].iFlap) == 0)
                    Stalling = true;
          }

          // Keep a running total of these resultant forces (total force)
          Fb += vResultant;

          // Calculate the moment about the CG of this element's force
          // and keep a running total of these moments (total moment)
          vtmp = Element[i].vCGCoords^vResultant;
          Mb += vtmp;
     }

     // Now add the thrust
     Fb += Thrust;

     // Convert forces from model space to earth space
     Airplane.vForces = QVRotate(Airplane.qOrientation, Fb);

     // Apply gravity (g is defined as −32.174 ft/s^2)
     Airplane.vForces.z += g * Airplane.fMass;

     Airplane.vMoments += Mb;
}

8637The first thing this function does is reset the variables that hold the total force and moment acting on the aircraft. 8638 Next, the thrust vector is set up. 8639 This is trivial in this example since we're assuming that the thrust vector always points in the plus x-axis direction (toward the nose) and passes through the aircraft center of gravity (so it does not create a moment).

8641After calculating the thrust vector, the function loops over the model elements to calculate the lift and drag forces on each element. 8642 We've skipped the fuselage in this model; however, if you want to account for its drag in your model, this is the place to add the drag calculation.

8644Going into the loop, the first thing the function does is check to see if the current element is element number six, the tail rudder. 8645 If it is, then the rudder's normal vector is recalculated based on the current incidence angle. 8646 The incidence angle for the rudder is altered when you press the X or C keys to apply rudder action.

8648The next calculation is to determine the relative velocity between the air and the element under consideration. 8649 As we stated earlier, this relative velocity consists of the linear velocity as the airplane moves through the air plus the velocity of each element due to the airplane's rotation. 8650 Once you've obtained this vector, you calculate the relative air speed by taking the magnitude of the relative velocity vector.

8652The next step is to determine the direction in which drag will act. 8653 Since drag opposes motion, it acts inline with, but opposite to, the relative velocity vector; thus, all you need to do is take the negative of the relative velocity vector and normalize the result (divide it by its magnitude) to obtain the drag direction vector. 8654 Since this vector was normalized, its length is equal to 1 (unity), so you can multiply it by the drag force that will be calculated later to get the drag force vector.

8656After obtaining the drag direction vector, this function uses it to determine the lift direction vector. 8657 The lift force vector is always perpendicular to the drag force vector, so to calculate its direction you first take the cross product of the drag direction vector with the element normal vector and then cross the result with the drag direction vector again. 8658 Here again, the function normalizes the lift direction vector.

8660Now that the lift and drag direction vectors have been obtained, the function proceeds to calculate the angle of attack for the current element. 8661 The attack angle is the angle between the lift vector and the element normal. 8662 You can calculate the angle by taking the inverse cosine of the vector dot product of the lift direction vector with the element normal vector. 8663 Since the drag vector is perpendicular to the lift vector, you can get the same result by taking the inverse sine of the vector dot product of the drag direction vector with the element normal vector.

8665Now with all the lift and drag vector stuff out of the way, the function goes on to calculate the resultant force acting on the element. 8666 The resultant force vector is simply the vector sum of the lift and drag force vectors. 8667 Notice that this is where the lift and drag coefficient functions are called and where the empirical lift and drag formulas previously discussed are applied.

8669After calculating the resultant force, the function checks to see if the calculated lift coefficient is 0. 8670 If it is, then the stall flag is set to warn us that the plane is in a stalled situation.

8672Finally, the resultant force is accumulated in the total force vector variable, and we calculate the moment by taking the cross product of the element coordinate vector with the resultant force. 8673 The resulting moment is accumulated in the total moment vector variable. 8674 After exiting the loop, the function adds the thrust vector to the total force.

8677So far, all of these forces and moments have been referenced in the body-fixed-coordinate system. 8678 The only thing left to do now is apply the gravity force, but this force acts in the negative y-axis direction in the earth-fixed-coordinate system. 8679 To apply the gravity force, the function must first rotate the body force vector from body space to earth space coordinates. 637 We used a quaternion rotation technique in this example, which we already discussed in 638Chapter 11638 and 640Chapter 128683 .
