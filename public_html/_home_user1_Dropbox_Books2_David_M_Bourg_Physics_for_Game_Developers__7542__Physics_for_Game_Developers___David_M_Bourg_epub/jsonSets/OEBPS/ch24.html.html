1Chapter 24. 3D Display

2For all the work 12681we've done to make programs' graphics more realistic, the best we can do is project our realistic simulations onto a two-dimensional screen. 12682 Although graphics libraries such as Microsoft DirectX and OpenGL can provide photorealistic renderings in real time, they still lack the ability to truly immerse the user in the works you have so carefully created. 12683 Three-dimensional display is something that the entertainment industry has attempted to make standard for some time. 12684 In reality, almost all 12685 "three-dimensional" 8 display technologies are what are technically 10called 10stereoscopic12688 displays. 12689 These displays use the way in which your eyes perceive depth to trick your brain into thinking it is seeing a three-dimensional image while the display remains two-dimensional. 12 In contrast, displays that actually involve creating a rendering in three dimensions are 13called 13volumetric12692 displays. 12693 We'll cover these later as part of our effort to discuss emerging technologies.

18Binocular Vision

20The trick to 12700displaying objects so that they appear to be three-dimensional depends on the method by which the human brain perceives the world around it. 23 Indeed, animals that have two eyes engage in what is called 23binocular vision12701 . 12702 Because each eye is in a slightly different position relative to the objects it is viewing, the left and right eye provide an image that is distinct to the brain. 25 This is called 26binocular disparity12705 . 12706 There are three possible results when the brain encounters these two different images: suppression, fusion, or summation. 29 29Suppression29 is 30when the brain ignores one of the images, 30summation30 is 32when the brain tries to perceive both images at the same time (double vision), and 33finally 33fusion12712 is mixing the two images to create a depth of field. 12713 The process of binocular fusion is something our brain learns to do when we are first born.

38Note

12718Given the way that eyes focus light, we are born seeing the world upside down! 12719 After a few days, our brains instinctively flip the images over so that the motion of our hands matches the motion that we observe. 12720 There have even been tests where people wearing glasses that invert your vision will eventually see the images right side up. 12721 When they take off the glasses, everything looks upside down again until their brain has time to correct the image.

12724Binocular fusion is also a learned behavior of the brain. 12725 The visual cortex takes the independent visual information from each eye and fuses it into a single image. 12726 Your brain does this as a way of organically calculating the distance to objects so that you can efficiently interact with the three-dimensional world. 12727 The exact process by which your brain accomplishes this is an area of active research. 12728 In fact, researchers have found that two images need not have any geometrical disparity in order to be fused. 12729 That is, if you take the exact same photograph of the same object and the same angle, but with different lighting, the shadows being cast can also cause the brain to recreate the object in three dimensions.

54Parallax54 is the 12733distance an object moves between the left- and right-eye images. 12734 You can easily demonstrate it by holding your thumb six inches from your face and closing one of your eyes. 12735 Block some of the words on this page with your thumb. 12736 Now open that eye and close the other one. 12737 The words that were behind your thumb should now be visible. 12738 This is because your eyes are not in the same position, so the different angles provide slightly different pictures of the page. 12739 This distance your thumb appeared to move is the parallax at that distance from your eye.

77The best way to view the stereopair shown in 78Figure 24-112757 is to begin by looking above this book at a far-off object. 12758 Now lower your gaze without refocusing your eyes and stare between the two sets of circles. 12759 With some trying, your brain should be able to fuse the images so that there are now three sets of circles. 12760 The original two will be out of focus, and the center set should appear to be three-dimensional. 12761 You can also get the sets to fuse by crossing your eyes; however, this is much less comfortable than using your eyes in their distance-viewing configuration.

12763Given that your brain is excellent at real-time pattern recognition, it can also compare visual information over time to get a sense of size and relative distance. 86 This is 87called 87movement parallax12766 , and it causes objects that are closer to you to appear to move faster when you move your head than objects that are farther away. 12767 For example, if you are driving in a car, you'll notice that the trees appear to move faster than the moon. 12768 This is because the trees are very close in comparison to the moon. 12769 Your brain uses this apparent speed disparity to help conclude that the moon is very far away indeed. 12770 In the next chapter we'll discuss how computer algorithms attempt this sort of pattern recognition.

95In fact, according to 95Flight Simulation12773 (edited by J. M. Rolfe and K. J. Staples; Cambridge University Press), the process of 3D visualization depends on the following eight major factors.

  • 98Occlusion of one object by another

  • 100Subtended visual angle of an object of known size

  • 102Linear perspective (convergence of parallel edges)

  • 104Vertical position (objects higher in the scene generally tend to be perceived as farther away)

  • 106Haze, desaturation, and a shift to bluishness

  • 108Change in size of textured pattern detail

  • 110Stereopsis

  • 112Accommodation of the eyeball112 (eyeball focus)

12791A standard 3D graphics library is capable of giving the appearance of three dimensions on the screen, just as any good painter on a canvas. 12792 Both standard 3D libraries and painters do their job by recreating the first six items in the preceding list. 12793 To further the illusion, 3D display technology simulates the seventh, stereopsis. 116 Stereopsis 12795is impression of depth generated by the fact that you have two eyeballs looking at slightly different angles. 12796 In short, the graphics library renders two different images, one for each eye, that have a parallax shift. 12797 These images are then delivered to each eye separately. 12798 The method by which the images are segregated varies from technology to technology. 12799 We will discuss these in a little bit.

12802The last item on the list, accommodation of the eyeball, is the process by which your eye changes shape to focus at different distances. 12803 By correlating the shape of your eye with the distance to the object, accommodation works as one of the pieces of information your brain uses to determine depth. 12804 As current 3D displays still use a 2D screen, the eyes are still focusing on the same plane regardless of the object's perceived depth; therefore, the eighth item in the list is not recreated. 12805 This is why most 3D displays still do not seem completely real. 12806 Some technologies, such as holograms and volumetric displays, allow for accommodation of the eyeball, but usually at the expense of some other factor. 129 We'll touch on these beyond state-of-the-art technologies near the end of the 12809chapter.

134Stereoscopic Basics

136There are 12816some extra considerations when it comes using today's 3D display technologies to recreate the images that would usually be provided to the visual cortex by binocular vision. 12817 Normally two eyes create two images that the brain combines with a biological depth map. 12818 The earliest stereoscopic images were generated in the 1800s from two photographs taken from slightly different positions. 12819 The viewer would then look at the photos through what came to be known as a stereoscope. 12820 This device was essentially an early example of the View-Master that some of you might remember from childhood. 12821 While the principle of showing unique images to each eye is straightforward in this case, it doesn't allow group viewing and requires that the user have something pressed against his eyes. 12822 To make 3D display something that a group of people can all experience together and in some cases even without the aid of any headgear, we must look at some more sophisticated methods of segregating the right and left images.

233Types of Display

235As just explained, 12914 3D display technology depends on providing two distinct images, one to each eye. 12915 In the next sections we'll discuss the common techniques used today.

240Complementary-Color Anaglyphs

242Anyone who saw a 3D movie in the 80s 12922remembers the cheap red and blue glasses one had to wear to get the effect. 245 These were 245complementary-color anaglyphs12923 . 12924 An anaglyph is the technique of encoding the separate images in a single photograph or video frame using color filters. 12925 The method calls for two horizontally shifted images to be viewed simultaneously. 12926 The images will contain the two images tinted in opposite colors of the scheme. 12927 While there are many color combinations that can be used, the most common today are red and cyan. 12928 These colors are chosen because the cyan and red filters are the most exclusive. 12929 Red and green filters were used earlier, but the green filter allows too much red light to leak through. 252 This can 253cause what is called 253binocular rivalry12932 , where your brain has a hard time figuring out which depth map to use. 255 One way to illustrate this is via the simple drawing of a transparent cube, as shown in 256Figure 24-612935 .

263Figure 24-6. Cube demonstrating binocular rivalry

265If you focus on the cube in 266Figure 24-612945 , your brain may start to flip between interpreting the upper face as forward of the lower face, and the lower face being forward. 12946 While this is caused by incomplete depth cues, the same uneasy feeling can be caused when your brain receives leaks across the two channels in a stereoscopic display. 12947 As you can imagine, this would be pretty annoying during a video game.

12949As the glasses don't require any electronics to do this, it is an example of passive 3D technology. 12950 The major drawback of this method is that the red component of the images is muted to the viewer. 12951 There are many improvements that can be made to the system to correct the color and account for some fuzziness. 12952 One example is the patented ColorCode 3D, which uses amber and blue filters. 12953 The advantage of this system is a nearly full color space and a fairly good image when not viewed with the glasses.

12955Anaglyphs fell out of favor with movie and game producers when polarization techniques came into maturity. 12956 These produce better color reproduction and reduce eyestrain. 12957 However, given the relatively inexpensive glasses required and that nothing special is required of the display other than that it be capable of displaying colors, anaglyphs have had a resurgence in printed material and online.

281Linear and Circular Polarization

282As polarized light plays a very12964 important part in the largest 3D displays, movie screens, we'll review what polarization of light means and how to accomplish it. 12965 Light can be thought of as an electromagnetic wave traveling through space. 12966 Let's begin our discussion by considering a common light bulb. 12967 It emits electromagnetic waves in all directions and is nominally 12968 "white." 291 An electromagnetic wave 12970oscillates perpendicularly to its line of travel. 293 This is 294called a 294transverse wave12973 . 12974 In comparison, sound waves oscillate in the same direction they travel, creating regions of higher density. 297 These 298are called 298longitudinal waves12977 . 12978 Only transverse waves can be polarized because only transverse waves have oscillation in multiple orientations. 12979 Going back to our light bulb, it is emitting 12980 "dirty" 12981 light in that the electromagnetic waves are all at random orientations.

12984Most sources of electromagnetic radiation (i.e., light) are composed of many molecules that all have different orientations when they emit light, so the light is unpolarized. 12985 If that light passes through a polarization filter, it leaves the filter with the oscillations in only one direction. 12986 In fact, there are two types of filters. 309 309Linear filters 12988produce oscillations in one direction. 311 311Circular filters12989 (a special case of elliptical filters) create circularly polarized light that rotates in an either righthand or lefthand direction. 12990 As circular filters depend on linear filters first, we'll discuss those now.

315The simplest and most common linear filter is the 12994wire-grid polarizer. 317 Imagine many very fine wires running parallel to one another with small gaps between them, as shown in 318Figure 24-712997 . 12998 When unpolarized light hits the wires, the oscillations that are parallel to the waves excite the electrons in the wire and move them along the length of the wire. 12999 This phenomenon causes that component of the oscillations to be reflected. 13000 However, the electrons cannot easily move perpendicular to the length of the wires, so the reflection phenomenon doesn't occur. 13001 What we are left with on the far side of the filter is a beam of light with the oscillations all in the same direction.

329Figure 24-7. Wire-grid polarization

13009Early 3D display systems used linearly polarized light to separate the right eye channel from the left eye channel. 13010 However, there is one problem with using linear polarizers. 13011 It follows that if you place another wire-grid polarizer after the first, with its wires rotated 90 degrees, no light will pass through! 13012 In fact, if you have an old pair of sunglasses or 3D glasses and you hold the right eye lens against the left eye lens, you won't be able to see anything. 13013 That is because each filter is blocking out one direction of oscillations, preventing any light from coming through. 13014 If you rotated one of the lenses, then the combined lens will lighten as you align the polarization directions. 13015 The problem with these types of lenses is that if you were watching a movie and tilted your head to one side, the same effect would occur and the image would be greatly dimmed. 13016 This means your date could no longer rest his or her head on your shoulder while watching the movie. 13017 Something had to be done.

13020Circular polarization is another form of filtering out certain orientations so that you can control which light beams pass through which lens. 13021 However, in this case the direction of oscillation is not a single orientation but more accurately a pattern of oscillations parameterized by time. 13022 The first step to achieve circular polarization is to send the light through a linear polarizer as just discussed. 345 That light is then sent through what is known as a 345quarter-wave filter13023 . 346 A typical arrangement is shown in 347Figure 24-813026 .

354Figure 24-8. Circular polarization filter (public domain image by Dave3457; 355http://commons.wikimedia.org355)

13035First a linear polarizer rotated to 45 degrees accepts incoming light and polarizes as we discussed earlier. 13036 The circular polarization effect is accomplished when a light wave polarized at 45 degrees hits the filter that accepts both 0- and 90-degree oscillations. 13037 As previously noted, this is called a quarter-wave filter. 13038 The resulting combination of 0- and 90-degree components of the intermediate 45-degree beam results in oscillations that turn right or left in a regular pattern. 13039 Patterns that turn counterclockwise are called left-handed. 13040 Patterns that turn clockwise are called right-handed.

13042The main benefit is that the lenses create the same pattern regardless of their rotation about the center of the lens. 365 In other words, if you rotated the assembly shown in 366Figure 24-813045 , meaning both lenses about the center axis, there will be no change in the polarization. 13046 This reduces the effect of head position on the viewer's ability to fuse the right and left eye channels, reducing eyestrain and increasing comfort. 13047 As a side note, it is also required for use in digital cameras, as linear polarization would affect the autofocus and light-metering features of SLRs.

13049Like anaglyphs, polarized 3D systems also use glasses to separate two channels that are projected at the same time. 13050 The first systems used two projectors, each with a different linear polarization filter projecting on to the same screen with precise timing. 13051 As the glasses would allow only the correctly polarized light to be seen by either eye, the viewer perceived binocular disparity. 13052 However, the precise timing between the projectors would be subject to errors that cause eyestrain and binocular rivalry. 13053 Newer systems, including RealD, use an active polarization filter fitted to the projector. 13054 However, this is still classified as a passive system, because the glasses the user has are just normal passive filters. 13055 In this system, there is a single filter that can change its polarization up to 200 times a second. 13056 Every other frame is separately polarized, and binocular disparity is experienced without the complexity of an additional projector. 13057 Although this system uses an active filter, the glasses don't have to actively change to separate the two channels, so this is another example of passive technology.

13059The main benefit of polarized systems over anaglyphs is that they provide full-color viewing and avoid binocular rivalry. 13060 This can increase the viewers' comfort when they are watching feature-length films. 13061 The disadvantages are cost and dimness. 13062 The glasses cost much more, and the complexity of projection is increased. 13063 It is impossible to create the effect in static media like print or web pages using standard displays. 13064 Also, because the lens on the projector is blocking out the portions of the light that don't have the correct polarization, the images appear dimmer to the viewer. 387 This can cause up to 30% reduction in brightness and is the main point of contention for many 13069directors.

394Liquid-Crystal Plasma

396The other display technologies 13075discussed were passive technologies. 13076 The projection carries the two channels and the glasses separate the channels, one for each eye, without active participation from the glasses. 13077 Active technologies require that the glasses do the work of separating the channels while the display is less important. 13078 As the gaming industry is more sensitive to adapting 3D display technologies to work with existing computer monitors or TVs, it has generally focused on active technologies. 401 The most common active technologies are based 403on 403liquid-crystal shutter glasses13081 , or LC glasses. 13082 The LC glasses work by exploiting a property of liquid crystals that causes them to turn black when a voltage is applied to them. 13083 This is the same technology that creates the eight-segment digits on a simple calculator.

13086Basically, every other frame being displayed is shown only to one eye, as the LC glasses cause the lens to darken when the opposing eye's frame is being displayed. 13087 To make sure the glasses are preventing the correct image from being seen by the corresponding eye, the computer broadcasts a timing signal to the glasses either over a wire or wirelessly. 13088 At the appropriate time, the right eye lens has a voltage applied to it and the entire lens quickly turns black. 13089 As light can enter only the left eye, that eye sees the image on the screen. 13090 As the video being displayed moves to the next frame, this time for the right eye, the glasses simultaneously are triggered to remove the voltage from the right lens and apply it to the left lens. 13091 With the left lens now darkened, only the right eye sees the right eye image. 13092 As long as this is happening very quickly,—on the order of 60 times per second per eye, or a total refresh rate of 120 Hz—your brain can't detect that only one eye is seeing the information on the display at a time. 13093 Instead, it interprets it as each eye seeing distinct images continuously, and as long as the image follows the rules we discussed earlier, it interprets it as having depth.

13096As you can imagine, the main disadvantage of this technology for gaming would be that you have to ensure the frame rate stays relatively high. 13097 You are now rendering twice as many images as you normally would require. 13098 We'll discuss more about the rendering pipeline later. 13099 Also related to frame rate is the refresh rate of the display. 13100 As each eye is really seeing only half the frames, the overall frame rate is half whatever the refresh rate is on the screen. 13101 Older displays have refresh rates at 60 Hz and effectively halving that can create issues with eyestrain; however, new displays support 120 Hz refresh rates so that halving it still allows for smooth display. 13102 Also, the display will seem much dimmer with the glasses on, as your eyes are seeing, on average, only half the light they normally would. 13103 As you can see, dimness is a common problem among 3D display technologies.

13106The main advantage is that you don't need a special display. 13107 As long as your display is capable of the required refresh rate, then you can retrofit it with a pair of LC shutter glasses and get 3D display out of it. 13108 Nvidia released such a kit in 2008, called 3D Vision, that is relatively popular. 13109 Graphics cards are capable of automatically converting the depth of the object in the model world into a parallax so that older games can also be rendered in stereoscopy. 13110 This is something to consider as you design your next game and something we'll touch on again in a moment.

435Autostereoscopy

437While the newer 3D 13116technologies are a far cry from the 3D of several decades ago, they still rely on people wearing glasses to view the image. 13117 This means the displays can never be used in a casual setting such as an arcade or street advertisement. 13118 Also, the younger segment of the gaming population might break or lose the glasses. 13119 Having to put on glasses to view the 3D images also provides a signal to your brain that what you are about to view is optical illusion. 13120 Put on the glasses, and your brain is already thinking that this isn't really in 3D.

13123Autosterescopy endeavors to create the illusion of depth without the aid of any glasses or other wearable device. 446 The first and most common way it does this is by 447introducing a 447parallax barrier13126 between the display and the user. 13127 As discussed earlier, parallax—and by extension, binocular disparity—is what gives our brain a main source of information on depth. 13128 A parallax barrier uses the fact that each of your eyes sees things from a slightly different angle to separate the two channels required for stereoscopy. 13129 Physically, the barrier is a layer placed in or on the screen with a series of very precisely cut slits. 13130 Because your eyes are not in the same spot, the slits reveal different pixels on the screen to each eye. 453 A basic illustration of this method is shown in 454Figure 24-913133 .

461Figure 24-9. Parallax barrier

13141As shown in this figure, older screens used the slits to bar certain pixels from being seen by placing them above the screen. 13142 Newer screens like the one on the Nintendo DS place the barrier lower than the pixels, but before the backlight. 13143 This prevents your eyes from receiving the light from those pixels that are being shaded by the solid spaces between the slits. 13144 This results in a clearer image and a wider viewing angle.

13147Speaking of viewing angle, if the method works because your eyes aren't in the same spot, it is obvious that if you move your whole head then you'll also be seeing a different set of pixels. 13148 This is the drawback: that there is a relatively small area called the 13149 "sweet spot" 13150 that the user must position his head relative to the screen to perceive the 3D effect. 13151 It makes it inappropriate for movies, as only a portion of the seats would be in the sweet spot, but it is in use for handhelds where only one user will be viewing the screen at any given time. 13152 Another drawback is that because the slits are eliminating half the pixels from each eye, the system reduces the effective pixel count by one-half. 13153 This causes a reduction in resolution that can be countered by even higher pixel density.

13156Another method very similar to the parallax method is replacing the layer of slits with a series of lenses that direct light from certain pixels to a certain eye. 479 These are 480called 480lenticular lenses480 and are illustrated in 482Figure 24-1013160 .

488Figure 24-10. Lenticular lens screen

13168Here microscopic domed lenses are placed between the viewer and the screen. 13169 The lens focuses the light such that only certain pixels are seen by each eye, due to the slightly different angles by which the eyes view the lens. 13170 Benefits over the parallax barrier are that the position of the user is less restricted and the image is brighter. 13171 With both parallax barriers and lenticular lens arrays, it is possible to retrofit current screens with removable slide-in-place filters that allow for viewing of 3D content designed for use with those filters. 13172 At the time of writing, several large TV manufacturers are doing active research into widening the field of view of these technologies for use in a home entertainment environment.

497Advanced Technologies

13177The displays we have discussed so far have all lacked some level of realism. 13178 For one, the eye doesn't have to refocus to observe objects at different depths, so your brain isn't totally fooled. 13179 Additionally, when you move around the object being projected and it doesn't change view, you still see the object at whatever angle the object was recorded. 13180 If you were viewing the world through a window, you could walk to the right and see more of the left-handed view. 13181 However, try as you might, you can't see around the corner of a building in a video game by moving your head at an angle of the screen. 13182 While it might be possible to recreate that effect with some sort of head tracking, there are some beyond-state-of-the-art technologies that could take this steps further.

507One technology that is commonly thought of being able to produce 3D images 508is the 508holograph13187 , a staple of science fiction. 13188 It seems like we should be able to just whip up some dynamic holographs and be done with screens all together. 13189 Who wouldn't like to play a sports game as if it were a table-top miniature? 13190 However, due to the way they are recorded, holographs as we know them are static images. 13191 Once recorded, holographic images cannot be changed. 13192 Due to their ability to encode multiple angles of viewing, they would make wonderful display technology, and research is under way to find a material that can hold holographic data and be rewritten fast enough to induce the illusion of motion to the viewer. 13193 From time to time, you may see in the news some event incorporating computer-generated imagery displayed in what is called holographic display. 13194 These are not true holographs, but usually just a projection on a semitransparent screen. 13195 The images are still completely flat, and the illusion of 3D comes solely from the brain not registering the presence of the screen.

520Another technology being actively 521researched is called 521integral photography13200 . 13201 This is a lot like the use of a lenticular lens; however, instead of linear cylinders in an array, the lens field is more like a fly's eye. 13202 Each lens in the array captures a complete picture from a slightly different angle. 13203 Now, when projected through a similar integral lens, the light forms a 4D field that the viewer sees as a 3D scene appropriate for his or her viewing angle. 13204 If the view moves to the side, then he or she will see a new portion of the object that wasn't visible previously. 13205 This type of movement parallax creates very realistic 3D experiences. 528 The advanced displays so accurately recreate the light that recorded the images that the eye can focus on different parts of the object (this is called the 528wave front13206 ) and therefore experience accommodation of the eyeball. 13207 Recall that this is the eighth item in our list from earlier in the chapter, and it is something the other displays are lacking. 13208 Some crude demonstrations of this technology have been presented, and it will be exciting to see how the research progresses.

13211Beyond any other method, the last one we'll talk about takes the bull by the horns. 13212 If you want a 3D image, just make the image three-dimensional. 13213 The other displays we discussed all attempt to recreate 3D screens using projection from a 2D surface. 536 There is a group of display technologies known as 536volumetric536 that 537dispense with any 2D elements and attempt to create a light field with well-defined 537x537, 537y537, and 537z13216 coordinates. 13217 These displays are far enough away from consumers that the definition of a volumetric display is still being argued. 540 One of the biggest problems with the technology will 541be 541occlusion13220 —that is, when an object passes in front of another object, you can't see the object behind the object that is closer to you. 13221 Pretty basic depth information, right? 13222 Well, if you are attempting to create a 3D light field, it is difficult to get the light to be blocked out when another rendered object passes in front of the original object. 13223 Simply not creating light there won't work, as each viewer would expect the farther object to be blocked at different angles. 13224 There are some existing demonstrators that use lasers to excite electrons in the air. 13225 When the lasers are focused on the same three-dimensional point, the combined energy creates a small pocket of plasma that gives off light. 548 These small volumes of light are often referred to 549as 549voxels13228 and correspond to pixels in 2D display technology. 551 The current resolution and refresh rate is not going to be wowing any gamers in the near future, but I for one can't wait for the day I can watch the New Orleans Saints play as a three-dimensional 13230table-top game.

555Programming Considerations

557Now that you 13236have a background in how the current 3D display technologies work, there are some aspects of each that you as the programmer should consider when writing games. 559 There are two ways to add 3D stereoscopic content to games: active stereoization and 13238passive stereoization. 13239 These are not to be confused with the passive and active technologies for viewing the 3D images. 13240 The stereoization process is the method by which the 3D images get created in the first place.

565Active stereoization565 is the567 process by which the programmer creates two cameras, rendering separate images for each 13247eye. 570 570Passive stereoization13248 removes the requirements for two cameras, and adds the stereoization at the GPU level. 13249 Either method is going to cost something in performance. 13250 The worst-case cost is twice a monocular scene; however, some elements of the scene, like the shadow map, will not require recalculation for each eye.

575Active Stereoization

13255Active stereoization is conceptually simpler and offers greater control over the process of stereoization. 13256 The most naive implementation is to simply have two cameras that render complete scenes and then pass the buffers labeled one for each eye. 13257 The buffers are then swapped in and out with the traditional definition of frame rate being half the actual frame rate. 13258 However, this simple implementation duplicates some elements of the scenes that are not eye dependent.

13261The advantage of this technique is precise control of what each eye is seeing. 13262 This allows the programmer to determine eye separation for each frame and could be used to actually disorient the user as a game element. 13263 Consider a flash-bang grenade going off: the programmer could alter the position of the cameras such that it would disorient the user in 3D for a short period after detonation. 13264 However, this technique would cause very real discomfort to the user, so it should be not used frequently!

13267The disadvantages are that the programmer is now responsible for managing an extra camera that must be rendered for each frame. 13268 For commercial titles, this method can be difficult considering that most games already have to be careful about how many times they invoke the render pipeline in order to maintain playable frame rates. 13269 Having to manage two cameras creates additional runtime burden on the program and makes the use of existing game engines a little more difficult.

594Also due to the fact that not everyone's eyes have the same 594separation594 (intraocular distance) 13274and not everyone's brain is willing to accept fabricated binocular disparity, the program must also provide options for the user to adjust the depth and complexity of the stereo effect. 13275 If you do not provide a way for the user to tune the experience, a vocal minority will claim your game gives them a splitting headache. 13276 However, as stated before, if you are a curious amateur, the process of moving the camera and rendering stereoscopic images gives you a lot of insight into how the process works.

13279As discussed previously, we must take into account the intraocular distance of the viewer. 13280 This is the amount of parallax we want to give objects at infinity. 13281 This distance usually ranges between 3 cm and 6.5 cm. 13282 The large differences can arise when considering that you must consider both adults and children when creating your 3D scene. 13283 Now it is useful to develop a normalized measure of intraocular distance. 606 Nvidia calls this 606real eye separation606 and gives the following formula for the value:

606Real eye separation = intraocular distance / real screen width

13286Note that this value will change depending on the width of the user's screen. 13287 This value is important because it is used as a reference for the maximum camera separation when rendering your stereoscopic images. 13288 Separations higher than this value will cause discomfort in the viewer. 13289 In fact, for computer screens where the user is relatively close to the screen, most people don't feel comfortable when the camera separation is more than half the real eye separation. 13290 This is why it is a good practice to allow the user to change the separation as a parameter of the program. 13291 It is also important to remember that the separation of the cameras is also the parallax value at an infinite distance. 13292 When two images display parallax equal to separation, it is as if your eyes are perfectly parallel. 13293 This is something that happens only when you are looking at something very far away, like a mountaintop. 13294 If you increase parallax beyond separation, you would essentially be asking your viewer to diverge her eyes beyond parallel. 13295 This is sort of the opposite of crossing your eyes, and obviously is going to cause some discomfort.

620As we now have an upper bound to our parallax, we can begin to create a 620parallax budget13298 . 621 Recall that the distance at which the two frustums intersect is called 13301convergence. 13302 Well, at 100 times this distance, the value of parallax is 99% of separation. 13303 That means that for objects in the scene being rendered at that depth value, they will appear flat and all similarly far away. 13304 This is analogous to not being able to tell which peak of a faraway mountain range is the closest.

13307Objects between 10 and 100 times the convergence distance have parallax that varies by about 10%. 13308 This results in a subtle but perceptible depth differentiation. 13309 As you approach convergence, the parallax exponentially decreases. 13310 At the convergence distance, the parallax is equal to 0. 13311 At a distance closer to the viewer than the screen, the parallax is negative. 13312 A distance out of the screen half that of the convergence distance creates a negative parallax that is equal to the separation. 13313 If the object is any closer to the viewer, her eyes verge on crossing and eyestrain becomes a problem. 636 Now we can draw what our budget looks like (see 637Figure 24-1113316 ).

13324The budget scales with convergence distance and separation. 13325 You should make sure as much of the important 3D action occurs between convergence and 10 times convergence. 13326 Your entire scene should be contained with negative convergence/2 to positive 100 times convergence.

13329In general, you must be most careful when trying to execute an out-of-screen effect.13332 These effects are very impressive to the viewer but cause the most eyestrain due to the rapid change in parallax. 13333 Having the object first appear farther away than the screen and then moving it closer to the user provides a context for the brain and encourages fusion. 13334 If an object is going to be closer to the user than the screen, it is also important to prevent that image from being clipped by the edge of the screen. 13335 That would make a portion of the 3D object disappear, and the clipping always occurs at the convergence point. 13336 This will give conflicting cues to the viewer and cause the 3D effect to be diminished. 13337 Given the amount of control you need in order to prevent out-of-screen effects from causing viewer discomfort, it is often best to use in non-player-controlled scenes.

13340Another difficult part of the game to render is the 2D elements. 13341 The user interface or other menu items that have no depth are normally rendered at convergence depth. 13342 However, there are some elements that are 2D but should be rendered at some nonzero depth. 13343 The most important of these are mouse pointers and crosshairs. 13344 These should be rendered at the depth of the object below them. 667 This change in depth of the user-controlled pointer helps maintain the idea that the objects are at different 13347depths.

672Passive Stereoization

674Passive stereoization takes 13354the responsibility for managing the stereoization process out of the programmer's hands. 13355 The programmer sends the render pipeline a single render command as usual, and the GPU handles generating the stereo images. 678 Most systems rely on 678heuristic subroutines13358 in the driver to take the monocular scene and generate binocular images. 13359 A heuristic subroutine is one that attempts to give a computer 13360 "common sense" 13361 about what it is trying to do to avoid having to do an exhaustive search for solutions to an existing problem. 13362 They are algorithms not based on rigid mathematical formulas but more like neural networks; they must be trained to do what you want them to. 13363 These algorithms decide which elements of the scene are eye dependent and which are not in a process that occurs entirely in the render pipeline.

13366It is possible for the programmer to defeat the 13367 "common sense" 13368 rules the computer is using in the pipeline so the method is not entirely fire-and-forget but does reduce a lot of the workload for development. 13369 One of the biggest benefits for larger game studios is that it avoids anyone having to reprogram existing game engines. 13370 By the same token, it allows existing games to be easily played in stereoscopic 3D. 13371 All of the preceding recommendations apply to passive stereoization except that there should be no user-adjustable settings in the game. 13372 Passive stereoization relies on third-party profiles that help the GPU do the work. 13373 The user will have set up a profile with whatever stereoization software he or she is using, such as NVIDIA's 3D Vision. 13374 Other recommendations may be specific to the stereoizer, and manufacturers usually publish a best practices guide. 697 The NVIDIA one is very helpful, and we recommend you read it if you are interested in using stereoization in your 13376games.
