1Preface

8Who Is This Book For?

4Simply put, this book is targeted at computer game developers who do not have a strong mechanics or physics background, charged with the task of incorporating 4real physics10 in their games. 5

12As a game developer, and very likely as a gamer yourself, you've seen products being advertised as 13 "ultra-realistic," 14 or as using 15 "real-world physics." 16 At the same time you, or perhaps your company's marketing department, are wondering how you can spice up your own games with such realism. 17 Or perhaps you want to try something completely new that requires you to explore real physics. 18 The only problem is that you threw your college physics text in the lake after final exams and haven't touched the subject since. 19 Maybe you licensed a really cool physics engine, but you have no idea how the underlying principles work and how They will affect what you're trying to model. 20 Or, perhaps you are charged with the task of tuning someone else's physics code but you really don't understand how it works. 21 Well then, this book is for you. 16

23Sure you could scour the Internet, trade journals, and magazines for information and how-to's on adding physics-based realism to your games. 24 You could even fish out that old physics text and start from scratch. 25 However, you're likely to find that either the material is too general to be applied directly, or too advanced requiring you to search for other sources to get up to speed on the basics. 26 This book will pull together the information you need and will serve as the starting point for you, the game developer, in your effort to enrich your game's content with physics-based realism. 21

28This book is not a recipe book that simply gives sample code for a miscellaneous set of problems. 29 The Internet is full of such example programs (some very good ones we might add). 30 Rather than give you a collection of specific solutions to specific problems, our aim is to arm you with a thorough and fundamental understanding of the relevant topics such that you can formulate your own solutions to a variety of problems. 31 We'll do this by explaining, in detail, the principles of physics applicable to game development, and by providing complimentary hand calculation examples in addition to sample programs. 26

27What We Assume You Know

34 Although we don't assume that you are a physics expert, we do assume that you have at least a basic college level understanding of classical physics typical of non-physics and non-engineering majors. 35 It is not essential that your physics background is fresh in your mind as the first several chapters of this book review the subjects relevant to game physics. 30

37We also assume that you are proficient in trigonometry, vector, and matrix math, although we do include reference material in the appendices. 38 Further, we assume that you have at least a basic college level understanding of calculus, including integration and differentiation of explicit functions. 39 Numerical integration and differentiation is a different story, and we cover these techniques in detail in the later chapters of this book. 34

35Mechanics

42 Most people that we've talked to when we was developing the concept for this book immediately thought of flight simulators when the phrases 43 "real physics" 44 and 45 "real-time simulation" 46 came up. 47 Certainly cutting edge flight simulations are relevant in this context; however, many different types of games, and specific game elements, stand to benefit from physics-based realism. 42

49Consider this example: You're working on the next blockbuster hunting game complete with first-person 3D, beautiful textures, and an awesome sound track to set the mood, but something is missing. 50 That something is realism. 51 Specifically, you want the game to 52 "feel" 53 more real by challenging the gamer's marksmanship, and you want to do this by adding considerations such as distance to target, wind speed and direction, and muzzle velocity, among others. 54 Moreover, you don't want to fake these elements, but rather, you'D like to realistically model them based on the principles of physics. 55 Gary Powell, with MathEngine Plc, put it like this 56 "The illusion and immersive experience of the virtual world, so carefully built up with high polygon models, detailed textures and advanced lighting, is so often shattered as soon as objects start to move and interact."51[52152]59 "It's all about interactivity and immersiveness," 60 says Dr. Steven Collins, CEO of Havok.com.55[56256]63 We think both these guys or right on target. 64 Why invest so much time and effort making your game world look as realistic as possible, but not take the extra step to make it behave just as realistically? 59

60Here are a few examples of specific game elements that stand to benefit, in terms of realism, from the use of real physics:

  • 61The trajectory of rockets and missiles including the effects of fuel burn off

  • 63The collision of objects such as billiard balls

  • 64The effects of gravitation between large objects such as planets and battle stations

  • 66The stability of cars racing around tight curves

  • 67The dynamics of boats and other waterborne vehicles

  • 69The flight path of a baseball after being struck by a bat

  • 70The flight of a playing card being tossed into a hat

77This is by no means an exhaustive list, but just a few examples to get you in the right frame of mind, so to speak. 78 Pretty much anything in your games that bounces around, flies, rolls, slides, or isn't sitting dead still can be realistically modeled to create compelling, believable content for your games. 73

80So how can this realism be achieved? 75 By using Physics, of course, which brings us back to the title of this section, the subject of mechanics . 758182 Physics is a vast field of science that covers many different, but related subjects. 83 The subject most applicable to realistic game content is the subject of mechanics, which is really what's meant by 84 "real Physics." 79

86By definition, mechanics is the study of bodies at rest and in motion, and of the effect of forces on them. 81 The subject of mechanics is subdivided into 81statics81, which specifically focuses on bodies at rest, and 81dynamics87 , which focuses on bodies in motion. 88 One of the oldest and most studied subjects of physics, the formal origins of mechanics can be traced back more than 2000 years to Aristotle. 83 An even earlier treatment of the subject was formalized in 83Problems of Mechanics89 , but the origins of this work are unknown. 90 Although some of these early works attributed some physical phenomena to magical elements, the contributions of such great minds as Galileo, Kepler, Euler, Lagrange, d'Alembert, Newton, and Einstein, to name a few, have helped develop our understanding of this subject to such a degree that we have been able to achieve the remarkable state of technological advancement that we see today. 85

92Because you want your game content to be alive and active, we'll primarily look at bodies in motion and will thus delve into the details of the subject of dynamics. 87 Within the subject of dynamics there are even more specific subjects to investigate, namely, 87kinematics87, which focuses on the motion of bodies without regard to the forces that act on the body, and 87kinetics93 , which considers both the motion of bodies and the forces that act on or otherwise affect bodies in motion. 94 We'll take a very close look at these two subjects throughout this book. 89

90Digital Physics

97This book's first edition focused exclusively on mechanics. 92 More than a decade after its release we've broadened our definition of game physics to include 92digital physics98 not in the cosmological sense but in the context of the physics associated with such devices as smart phones and their unique user interaction experience. 99 As more platforms such as the Wii, PlayStation, X Box and smart phones come out and are expanded developers will have to keep up with and understand the new input and sensors technologies that accompany these platforms in order to keep producing fresh gaming experiences. 100 But you shouldn't look at this as a burden, and instead look at it as an opportunity to enhance the user's interactive experience with your games. 95

96Arrangement of This Book

103 Physics-based realism is not new to gaming, and in fact many games on the shelves these days advertise their physics engines. 104 Also, many 3D modeling and animation tools have physics engines built in to help realistically animate specific types of motion. 105 Naturally, there are magazine articles that appear every now and then that discuss various aspects of physics-based game content. 100 In parallel, but at a different level, research in the area of real-time rigid body100[1013101]108 simulation has been active for many years, and the technical journals are full of papers that deal with various aspects of this subject. 109 You'll find papers on subjects ranging from the simulation of multiple, connected rigid bodies to the simulation of cloth. 110 However, while these are fascinating subjects and valuable resources, as we hinted earlier, many of them are of limited immediate use to the game developer as they first require a solid understanding of the subject of mechanics requiring you to learn the basics from other sources. 111 Further, many of them focus primarily on the mathematics involved in solving the equations of motion and don't address the practical treatment of the forces acting on the body or system being simulated. 106

113We asked John Nagle, with Animats, what is, in his opinion, the most difficult part of developing a physics-based simulation for games and his response was developing numerically stable, robust code.108[1094109]116 Gary Powell echoed this when he told me that minimizing the amount of parameter tuning to produce stable, realistic behavior was one of the most difficult challenges. 117 We agree; speed and robustness in dealing with the mathematics of bodies in motion are crucial elements of a simulator. 118 And on top of that, so are completeness and accuracy in representing the interacting forces that initiate and perpetuate the simulation in the first place. 119 As you'll see later in this book, forces govern the behavior of objects in your simulation and you need to model them accurately if your objects are to behave realistically. 114

121This prerequisite understanding of mechanics and the real world nature of forces that may act on a particular body or system have governed the organization of this book. 116 Generally, this book is organized in four parts with each building on the material covered in previous parts:

118Part I

119A mechanics refresher, comprising Chapters 1201120 through 1226128 .

124Chapter 1, 124Basic Concepts

131This warm up chapter covers the most basic of principles that are used and referred to throughout this book.  The specific topics addressed include mass and center of mass, Newton's Laws, inertia, units and measures, and vectors. 126

128Chapter 2, 128Kinematics

135This chapter covers such topics as linear and angular velocity, acceleration, momentum, and the general motion of particles and rigid bodies in two and three dimensions.

132Chapter 3, 132Force

139The principles of force and torque are covered in this chapter, which serves as a bridge from the subject of kinematics to that of kinetics.  General categories of forces are discussed including drag forces, force fields, and pressure.

136Chapter 4, 136Kinetics

137This chapter combines elements of Chapters 1382138 and 1403146 to address the subject of kinetics and explains the difference between kinematics and kinetics.  Further discussion treats the kinetics of particles and rigid bodies in two and three dimensions.

143Chapter 5, 143Collisions

150In this chapter we'll cover particle and rigid body collision response, that is, what happens after two objects run in to each other.

147Chapter 6, 147Projectiles

154This chapter will focus on the physics of simple projectiles laying the ground work for further specific modeling treatment in later chapters. 149

151Part II

152An introduction to real time simulations, comprising Chapters 1537153 through 15514161 .

158Chapter 7, 158Real-Time Simulations

165This chapter will introduce real-time simulations and detail the core of such simulations—the numerical integrator. 166 Various methods will be presented and coverage will include stability and tuning. 161

163Chapter 8, 163Particles

170Before diving into rigid body simulations, this chapter will show how to implement a particle simulation, which will be extended in the next chapter to include rigid bodies.

167Chapter 9, 1672D Rigid-Body Simulator

174This chapter will extend the particle simulator from the previous chapter showing how to implement rigid bodies, which primarily consists of adding rotation and dealing with the inertia tensor.

171Chapter 10, 171Implementing Collision Response

178Collision detection and response will be combined to implement real‐time collision capabilities in the 2D simulator.

175Chapter 11, 175Rotation in 3D Rigid-Body Simulators

182This chapter will address how to handle rigid body rotation in 3D including how to deal with the inertia tensor. 183 Then we'll show the reader how to extend the 2D simulator to 3D.

180Chapter 12, 1803D Rigid-Body Simulator

187Multiple unconnected bodies will be incorporated in the simulator in this chapter. 188 Introduction of multiple bodies requires resolution of multiple rigid body collisions, which can be very tricky. 189 Issues of stability and realism will be covered. 184

186Chapter 13, 186Connecting Objects

193Taking things a step further, this chapter will show how to join rigid bodies forming connected bodies, which may be used to simulate human bodies, complex vehicles that may blow apart, among many other game objects. 194 Various connector types will be considered. 189

191Chapter 14, 191Physics Engines

198In this chapter, specific aspects of automobile performance are addressed, including aerodynamic drag, rolling resistance, skidding distance, and roadway banking.

195Part III

196 A look at some real world problems, comprising Chapters 19715197 through 19919205 .

202Chapter 15, 202Aircraft

209This chapter focuses on the elements of flight including propulsor forces, drag, geometry, mass, and most importantly lift.

206Chapter 16, 206Ships and Boats

213The fundamental elements of floating vehicles are discussed in this chapter, including floatation, stability, volume, drag, and speed. 208

210Chapter 17, 210Cars and Hovercraft

217In this chapter, specific aspects of automobile performance are addressed, including aerodynamic drag, rolling resistance, skidding distance, and roadway banking. 218 Additionally hovercraft shares some of the same characteristics of both cars and boats. 213 This chapter will consider those characteristics that distinguish the hovercraft as a unique vehicle.  Topics covered include hovering flight, aerostatic lift, and directional control

215Chapter 18, 215Guns and Explosions

222This chapter will focus on the physics of guns including power, recoil, and projectile flight. 223 Since we generally want things to explode when hit with a large projectile, this chapter will also address the physics of and modeling explosions.

220Chapter 19, 220Sports

227This chapter will focus on the physics of ball sports such as baseball, golf, and tennis. 228 Coverage will go beyond projectile physics and include such topics as including pitching, bat swing, bat‐ball impact, golf club swing and club ball impact, plus tennis racket swinging and racket/ball impacts.

225Part IV

226Chapters in this part of the book will explain the physics behind accelerometers, touch screens, GPS and other gizmos showing the reader how to leverage these elements in their games, comprising Chapters 22720227 through 22926235 .

232Chapter 20, 232Touch Screens

239Touch screens facilitate virtual tactile interfaces with mobile device games, such as those made for the iPhone. 240 This chapter will explain the physics of touch screen and how the reader can leverage this interface in their games particularly with respect to virtual physical interaction with game elements through gesturing.

237Chapter 21, 237Accelerometers

244Accelerometers are now widely used in mobile devices and game controllers allowing virtual physical interaction between players and game objects. 245 This chapter will explain how accelerometers work, what data they provide and how that data can be manipulated with respect to virtual physical interaction with game elements. 246 Topics covered will include, but not be limited to integration of acceleration data to derive velocities and displacements and rotations.

243Chapter 22, 243Gaming from One Place to Another

250Mobile devices commonly have GPS capabilities and this chapter will explain the physics of the GPS system including relativistic effects. 251 Further, GPS data will be explained and this chapter showing the reader how to manipulate that data for virtual interaction with game elements. 252 For example, we'll show the reader how to differentiate GPS data to derive speed and acceleration among other manipulations. 247

249Chapter 23, 249Pressure Sensors and Load Cells

256Pressure sensing devices are used in games as a means of allowing players to interact with game elements, for example, the Wii balance board uses pressure sensors allowing players to interact with the Wii Fit game. 257 This chapter will explain the physics behind such pressure sensors, what data they generate, and how to manipulate that data for game interaction.

254Chapter 24, 2543D Display

261The new PlayStation Move and Microsoft's Kinect use optical tracking systems to detect the position of players' game controllers or gestures. 262 This chapter will explain the physics behind optical tracking and how to leverage this technology in games.

259Chapter 25, 259Optical Tracking

266As televisions and handheld game consoles race to implement 3D displays, several different technologies are being developed. 267 By understanding the physics of the glasses dependent stereoscopic displays, the new 268 "glasses free" 269 autostereoscopic displays, and looking forward to holography and volumetric displays, developers will be better positioned to leverage these effects in their games.

266Chapter 26, 266Sound

273Sound is a particularly important part of a game's immersive experience; however, to date no book on game physics addresses the physics of sound. 274 This chapter will focus on sound physics including such topics of sound speed and the Doppler Effect. 275 Discussions will also include why sound physics is often ignored in games, for example, when simulating explosions in outer space. 270

272Appendix A

279This appendix shows you how to implement a C++ class that captures all of the vector operations that you'll need to when writing 2D or 3D simulations. 274

276Appendix B

283This appendix implements a class that captures all of the operations you need to handle 3x3 matrices.

280Appendix C

287This appendix implements a class that captures all of the operations you need to handle quaternions when writing 3D rigid body simulations.

285Part I291 focuses on fundamental topics in Newtonian mechanics such as kinematics and kinetics. 292 Kinematics deals with the motion of objects. 293 We'll cover both linear and angular velocity and acceleration. 294 Kinetics deals with forces and resulting motion. 289 Part I serves as a primer for 290Part II297 that covers rigid body dynamics. 292 Readers already versed in classical mechanics can skip 293Part I300 without loss of continuity.

297Part II303 focuses on rigid body dynamics and development of both single and multi-body simulations. 304 This part covers numerical integration, real-time simulation of particles and rigid bodies, and connected rigid bodies. 305 Generally, this part covers what most game programmers consider elements of a physics engine. 300

302Part III308 focuses on physical modeling. 309 The aim of this part is to provide valuable physical insight for the reader so they can make better judgments on what to include in their models and what they can safely leave out without sacrificing physical realism. 310 We cannot and do not attempt to cover all the possible things you might want to simulate. 311 Instead we cover several typical things you may try to simulate in a game such as aircraft, boats, sports balls, among others with the purpose of giving you some insight into the physical nature of those things and some of the choices you must make when developing suitable models. 306

308Part IV314 covers digital physics in a broad sense. 315 This is an exciting topic as it relates to the technologies associated with mobile platforms, such as smart phones like the iPhone, and ground breaking game systems such as the Nentendo Wii. 316 Chapters in this part of the book will explain the physics behind accelerometers, touch screens, GPS and other gizmos showing the reader how to leverage these elements in their games. 317 We recognize that these topics are not what most game programmers typically think about when they think of game physics; however, the technologies covered play an increasingly important role in modern mobile games and we feel it important to explain the underlying physics behind them with the hope that you'll be better able to leverage these technologies in your games. 312
