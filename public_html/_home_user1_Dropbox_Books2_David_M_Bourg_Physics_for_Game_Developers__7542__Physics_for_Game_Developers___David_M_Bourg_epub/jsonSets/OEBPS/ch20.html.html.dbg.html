14Types of Touch Screens

16Resistive

18Resistive 11037touch screens are basically a giant network of tiny buttons. 11038 Some of them have 4,096 × 4,096 buttons in a single square inch! 11039 OK, so they are not quite just normal buttons, but they come close. 11040 Resistive touch screens have at least two layers of conductors with an air gap between them. 11041 As you press on the screen, you close the gap. 11042 Bam, circuit complete, button pressed. 11043 We will flesh out that simplified description shortly.

29Capacitive

31Also a topic we'll 11049soon discuss in detail, capacitive touch screens are very common on today's smartphones. 11050 These touch screens operate by calculating the change in electrical capacitance at the four corners of the screen when your finger influences the capacitive nature of the circuits under the glass. 11051 The limitation is that whatever touches the screen must be electrically conductive. 11052 If you insulate your fingers with gloves, the screen will no longer be able to locate your touch. 11053 However, this can be solved with a few stitches of conductive thread.

39Infrared and Optical Imaging

41Infrared touch 42screens use arrays of infrared 44LED 11062and photodetectors to detect and interpret an object breaking the path of a LED-photodetector pair. 11063 This uses line-scanning techniques and is a very robust design.

49Optical imaging 11067techniques are relative newcomers to the touch screen scene whose big advantage is that they are extremely scalable. 11068 They use imaging devices and light sources to detect where the screen is being touched by interpreting any shadows cast by an object through the thickness of the material.

54Exotic: Dispersive Signal and Surface Acoustic Wave

56Several other 11074exotic touch screen technologies exist. 58 We won't get into detail here, but 3M has a system that detects 11076mechanical energy in glass caused by a touch. 60 The amount of 11078vibration energy that reaches each sensor determines the position.

64Another example of exotic screen input, 11082surface acoustic wave technology detects changes in the pattern of ultrasonic waves traveling along the surface of the screen.

70Resistive Touch Screens

72Resistive touch screens 74are classified as a 74passive11091 touch screen technology because the screen registers a touch without any active participation by the object touching the screen. 75 This is their major benefit over 75active11092 technologies, such as capacitive touch screens, as resistive screens can be activated by nonconductive objects like a pen or gloved finger. 11093 In the past, resistive screens were limited to a single input, and that's the type we'll describe, but they can be made to work with two or more simultaneous inputs, also known as multitouch.

79One-dimensional resistive touch sensor

11098To ease ourselves into this discussion, we will begin by looking at a one-dimensional touch screen. 82 Let's imagine we have built the machine described in 83Figure 20-111101 .

90Figure 20-1. Linear resistive touch sensor

92As you can see, our sensor has two states, an 92open circuit state92 and a 92closed circuit state11109 . 11110 In the open circuit state, the controller is supplying a 5V signal to pin 2 and waiting for any return voltage on pin 1. 11111 With no touch to bring the wires together, the circuit is open. 11112 No voltage is present at pin 1, and therefore no touch is sensed. 11113 When the wires are touched, they are brought together and the circuit is closed. 11114 A voltage will then be present at pin 1. 11115 A touch event is registered.

101This type of sensor, which looks only for the presence or absence of voltage without regard to its value, is called a 101digital sensor11118 . 11119 It can detect only two states: on or off (1 or 0, respectively). 11120 OK, so it's not quite a touch screen yet; essentially at this point all we have is a simple button. 104 Moving forward, let's say that we not only want to trigger an event when we press our button, but we also want to simultaneously input a value based on the location along the wire, 104L11121 , that we pressed.

11124To accomplish this, the controller patiently waits for a voltage at pin 1. 11125 When it senses a voltage, that digital 11126 "on" 110 switch causes the controller to then probe the voltage that is present, which we have labeled 110V110X11127 . 111 Now we get to the reason it is called a 111resistive11128 touch sensor. 11129 Current, voltage, and resistance are all interrelated by Ohm's law. 113 This physical relationship is expressed as:

113V = IR

115Where 115V115 is voltage, 115I115 is current, and 115R11132 is resistance. 11133 The exact physical meaning of each of these is less important right now than how they are related, so don't get too worried if you can't recall their definitions. 117 In the case of our circuit, 117I11134 , or current, is going to be constant. 118 As the controller measures the voltage, 118V118, at pin 1, we can now solve for resistance:

118R = V/I

120With our constant current, 120I120, and our known voltage, 120V120X120, we can calculate the resistance of the circuit by inputting (5 – 120V120X120) for 120V120 as follows:

120R = (5 – 120V120X120) / I (1)

11139Note that we have to have the change in voltage across our resistor (the wire), so be sure to use the difference between the two values. 123 Every conductor has an inherent internal resistance, and through testing we can determine what the resistance is, measured in ohms per unit length, and use that to determine our total resistance described by:

123R = 2rL

125where 125r125 is the aforementioned ohms per unit length and 125R11142 is the total resistance of our circuit. 126 Note that we have multiplied 126L11143 by 2 to account for both the wire run to the point of contact and back. 127 If we substitute this for 127R127 in our earlier equation, we now have:

1272rL = (5 – 127V127X127) / I

129And finally:

129L = (5 – 129V129X129) / (2rI)

131where 131L11148 is the only unknown. 11149 To illustrate, let us assume the measured voltage is 4.95V and the wires are 24-gauge copper wires. 11150 A quick look in a standard electrical engineering book gives the resistance as 0.08422 ohms per meter. 134 When we designed our constant current source, let's say we picked 50 milliamps:

134L = (5 – 4.95V) / ((2)(.08422 ohms/meter)(.05A))
134L = 5.9 meters from the controller

11153As you can see, the material's resistance per meter, the constant current supplied, and the sensitivity of the voltage-sensing circuit must all be finely tuned to ensure that the controller is capable of sensing touch events in the appropriate dimensions. 11154 In a resistive touch screen, the wires are microscopic so that the resistance per meter is much higher. 11155 This allows the screen to detect smaller distances.

11237While the four-wire resistive touch screen is the simplest two-dimensional touch sensor, there are issues with durability. 11238 The main drawback of this type of touch screen is that, because the layers must be separated by an insulating gap, at least one of the layers must be flexible. 11239 In the four-wire type, the constant flexing of the first conductive layer introduces microcracks in the coating, which lead to nonlinearities and reduce the accuracy. 11240 Other models of resistive touch screens overcome this issue with additional layers that remove the need for the flexible conductor. 11241 They have also been adapted to provide multitouch capability. 225 We will discuss multitouch and how it works with capacitive touch screens in greater detail 11243shortly.

229Capacitive Touch Screens

231A capacitive screen 11249uses a piece of glass coated in a transparent conductor. 11250 When your finger or other conductor comes into contact with the screen, the electrostatic field is disturbed, causing a change in the capacitance. 11251 To understand how capacitive screens work, let's quickly review capacitance in general.

237A 237capacitor11254 in its simplest form is two conductors, usually thin plates, separated by an insulator. 11255 If you apply a voltage across the two conductors, a current will flow and charge will build up. 11256 Once the voltage across the plates is equal to the supply voltage, the current will stop. 11257 The amount of charge built up in the plates is what we measure as the capacitance. 11258 Previously, we noted that one issue with resistive screens is that one part must always flex to close a insulating gap and complete a circuit. 11259 This repetitive action eventually leads to mechanical failure. 11260 A capacitor can be dynamically formed by any two conductors separated by an insulator. 11261 Noting that glass is a good insulator, it is easy to see that a finger separated from a conductor by glass can change the capacitance of a system. 11262 In this way, the finger or stylus doesn't have to cause any mechanical action, yet it can still effect changes to the sensors, which are then used to measure the location of the touch.

248The methods of determining location based on capacitance on mobile devices are 248self-capacitance248 and 248mutual capacitance11265 .

251Self-capacitance

11270Anyone who has lived in a dry winter has felt the shock of a static electricity discharge. 11271 This zap is possible because the human body is a pretty good capacitor with a capacitance of about 22 pico-farads. 255 This property is known as 255body capacitance11272 . 11273 Self-capacitance screens take advantage of a physical property defined by the amount of electrical charge that must be added to an isolated conductor to raise its potential by one volt. 11274 When the fingers act as a conductor of the body's inherent capacitance, the sensors on the other side of the glass experience a rise in electric potential. 11275 Given that the sensors are on the other side of a good insulator, glass, there won't actually be any discharge of energy, unlike when you touch your metal car door and get 11276 "zapped." 11277 Self-capacitance in this manner produces a very strong signal but lacks the ability to accurately resolve multiple touches. 11278 Therefore, it is often used in conjunction with the next type of touch screen we'll discuss, mutual capacitance.

264Mutual capacitance

11283The other form of capacitance-sensing screen, mutual capacitance, is formed by a grid of independent capacitors. 11284 A probing charge is sent over the rows or columns. 11285 As the capacitors charge and discharge, the system can sense the capacitance of each individual capacitor. 11286 As just discussed, the body is a good capacitor, and bringing part of it close to the capacitor grid changes the local electric field. 11287 Those capacitors that are under a finger or other conductor will read lower values than normal. 11288 Each capacitor can be scanned independently, enabling high resolution of where the touch event is occurring. 11289 Additionally, because they act independently of one another, it is possible to accurately register multiple touches. 11290 Think of this system as taking a picture of the capacitance on the skin of the screen. 274 Using algorithms similar to image processing and edge detection, this system can compute the extent of a touch 11293event.

286The code for a 286Cocoa touch Objective-C286 event is as follows:

- (void)touchesBegan:(NSSet *)touches withEvent:(UIEvent *)event
{
    UITouch* touch = [[event touchesForView:self] anyObject];
    firstTouch = [touch locationInView:self];
    self.status = YES;
    [self trigger];
}

288where 288firstTouch288 is defined by 288CGPoint firstTouch;11305 in the header file. 289 The 289CGPoint289 is a Cocoa touch object capable of storing an (289x289,289y11306 ) coordinate in the display view's coordinate system. 290 We can then use 290firstTouch.x290 and 290firstTouch.y11307 later in our program to give a location to the particle explosion.

11309As you can see, it is very similar to a mouse-based event. 11310 One big difference is that you could adapt the code to handle multitouch events. 11311 Computers recognize only one mouse cursor at a time, but with a touch screen you can register multiple clicks simultaneously.

311Other Considerations

11330One of the major advantages of touch screens is that their layout and actions are entirely software based. 11331 That is to say, if a certain button does not pertain to the current layout, it can be discarded and the freed space used for additional relevant controls. 11332 We will discuss other less obvious considerations in the following sections.

318Haptic Feedback

320The flip 11339side of the advantage of not being locked into a set of physical buttons is that the user must rely almost entirely on vision to interact with the controls. 11340 At least one of the authors of this book uses a keyboard with no letters on it at all, instead relying totally on the physical position of the keys to determine which key to strike. 11341 This would be much harder without the tactile and audio cues to signify that the correct key has been pressed. 11342 Indeed, it is easy to tell when he is typing poorly because the backspace key is much louder than the rest!

328The method of including physical feedback to assist a user in interaction with entirely virtual objects is known as 328haptic feedback11345 . 329 The first use of haptic feedback in games was limited to arcade games such as 329Motocross11346 , in which the handlebars shook after an in-game impact. 11347 It is now considered standard on video game controllers, which vibrate to inform the user of some event.

11350In the realm of touch screens, haptic feedback is used to inform the user of a successful key strike or other touch-based event. 11351 Some touch screens even incorporate some movement of the entire screen when pressed. 11352 This feedback still doesn't allow touch typing, as it only dynamically responds and provides no static tactile feedback for different buttons.

338Modeling Touch Screens in Games

340Given their planar 11358nature and the lack of inherent haptic feedback, touch screens can be an easy way to implement controls with which a character in a game can interact. 11359 The amount of physical modeling required to create a realistic in-game keyboard is pretty intense. 11360 Thus, there are very few examples of an in-game character having to sit down and type a code in to a terminal via a standard keyboard.

11363By using touch screens for in-play control of objects, you can avoid an additional physical model while retaining realism. 11364 It would also be interesting to see games use realistic touch screen interfaces so that a character would have to remove his gloves to use a capacitive screen. 11365 Lastly, the exotic screen technologies mentioned earlier provide many creative avenues of modeling those types of screens in games. 11366 For example, for screens measuring sound waves in the glass or other mechanical energy, low-grade explosions could be used to trigger these in-game input devices.

352Difference from Mouse-Based Input

354One important 11373consideration for game developers in regards to touch screens is the difference from traditional mouse- and keyboard-based gaming. 11374 As console game developers have long been aware, it is hard to compete with the speed and accuracy of the mouse/keyboard combination. 11375 Many first-person shooters segregate their online gaming between controllers and mouse/keyboard setups, as the accuracy and speed of the mouse gives those players an unfair advantage. 11376 Upon using touch screens on many different gaming devices and mobile computing platforms, we feel that this advantage is even more pronounced.

11379A touch by a finger is an elliptical shape whose contact patch depends on the specific finger being used, the pressure applied, and the orientation of the finger. 11380 The user generally perceives the point of touch to be below where the actual center of contact is, so adjustments must be made. 11381 This is generally all handled automatically by the operating system so that a single touch point is computed and handed to the game via an API. 11382 However, this generic approach to computing touches must obviously sacrifice accuracy for universality so that it is not calibrated for one specific user.

11385Another inherent drawback to touch screens is the need to touch the screen. 11386 This means a large portion of your hand will be blocking the screen when you are controlling that element. 11387 One can imagine that in a first-person shooter, this would be a great disadvantage over someone who is playing with a keyboard and mouse.

11390Lastly, mouseover is not available to touch-screen-based input. 11391 Consider a game where you would trigger actions by merely moving a mouse cursor over an object. 11392 These actions could be distinct from clicking on the same object. 11393 However, with touch-screen-based input, that object would be obscured by whatever is triggering the screen, therefore rendering the mouseover action invisible to the user.
